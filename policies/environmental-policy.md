## 1. Environmental Policy Statement

**A Message from the Chief Executive Officer**

BuildRight Home Improvement, Inc. (BRHI) recognizes that the health of our environment is inseparable from the health of our business, our communities, and the customers we serve. As one of the nation's largest home improvement retailers, we have both an outsized responsibility and an extraordinary opportunity to drive positive environmental change across our operations, our supply chain, and the homes and communities our products help build and maintain.

We are committed to conducting our business in a manner that protects and preserves the environment for current and future generations. This commitment is not peripheral to our strategy — it is foundational. Every associate, at every level, shares in this responsibility.

Our environmental commitments are as follows:

- **Compliance and Beyond.** We will comply with all applicable environmental laws, regulations, and permits, and we will strive to exceed these requirements where doing so reduces environmental risk and creates long-term value.
- **Climate Action.** We will set and pursue science-based greenhouse gas reduction targets aligned with the goals of the Paris Agreement, including achieving net-zero emissions across our value chain by 2050.
- **Resource Stewardship.** We will reduce waste, conserve water, and improve energy efficiency across every store, distribution center, and office in our network.
- **Sustainable Products.** We will expand our offering of environmentally preferable products and provide our customers with the information they need to make sustainable choices for their homes.
- **Supply Chain Responsibility.** We will work with our suppliers to improve environmental performance throughout the product lifecycle, from raw material extraction through end of life.
- **Continuous Improvement.** We will maintain an ISO 14001-certified Environmental Management System and use data-driven approaches to measure, manage, and improve our environmental performance year over year.
- **Transparency.** We will publicly report our environmental performance, goals, and progress through annual sustainability reports and voluntary disclosures.

This policy establishes the framework within which every BRHI associate, contractor, and business partner must operate. I expect each of you to understand your role in advancing these commitments and to act on them every day.

*Marcus E. Caldwell*
*Chief Executive Officer*
*BuildRight Home Improvement, Inc.*

## 2. Scope

### 2.1 Organizational Scope

This policy applies to all operations, facilities, and activities of BuildRight Home Improvement, Inc. and its wholly owned subsidiaries, including but not limited to:

- All retail store locations (including garden centers and tool rental departments)
- Distribution centers and regional fulfillment hubs
- Corporate offices and shared service centers
- Direct delivery and transportation fleet operations
- Manufacturing and processing facilities operated by BRHI
- E-commerce fulfillment operations

### 2.2 Operational Scope

This policy governs environmental aspects across the full spectrum of BRHI's activities:

- **Direct operations:** Energy consumption, water use, waste generation, air emissions, chemical storage and handling, refrigerant management, land use, and transportation at all owned and leased facilities.
- **Supply chain:** Raw material sourcing, manufacturing processes, packaging, and transportation associated with products BRHI purchases and sells.
- **Product lifecycle:** Product design, durability, repairability, recyclability, and end-of-life considerations for products sold under BRHI-owned brands.
- **Contracted services:** Activities performed by third-party contractors, logistics providers, and service companies operating on BRHI premises or on BRHI's behalf.
- **New construction and renovations:** Design, construction, operation, and decommissioning of all new facilities and major renovation projects.

### 2.3 Geographic Scope

This policy applies to all BRHI operations within the United States, its territories, and any international operations. Where local regulations impose requirements more stringent than those set forth in this policy, the more stringent standard shall apply.

### 2.4 Exclusions

Franchise locations that are independently owned and operated are not bound by this policy but are encouraged to adopt its principles. BRHI will make environmental compliance resources available to franchise partners upon request.

## 3. Environmental Management System (EMS)

BRHI maintains an Environmental Management System certified to ISO 14001:2015. The EMS provides the systematic framework for identifying, managing, monitoring, and continuously improving BRHI's environmental performance across all operations.

### 3.1 Environmental Policy

This document serves as BRHI's environmental policy as defined by ISO 14001:2015 Clause 5.2. It:

- Is appropriate to the purpose and context of BRHI, including the nature, scale, and environmental impacts of our activities.
- Provides a framework for setting environmental objectives.
- Includes a commitment to the protection of the environment, including pollution prevention and other specific commitments relevant to BRHI's context.
- Includes a commitment to fulfill compliance obligations.
- Includes a commitment to continual improvement of the EMS to enhance environmental performance.
- Is maintained as a documented information, communicated within BRHI, and available to interested parties.

This policy is communicated to all persons working on behalf of BRHI, including contractors and temporary workers, during onboarding and through annual refresher communications. It is publicly available on BRHI's corporate website and in the Sustainability section of our annual report.

### 3.2 Planning

#### 3.2.1 Environmental Aspects and Impacts

BRHI identifies and evaluates environmental aspects — elements of its activities, products, and services that interact or can interact with the environment — using a systematic methodology:

- **Aspect identification** is performed at the facility level by cross-functional teams comprising store operations, facilities management, logistics, merchandising, and environmental compliance personnel.
- **Impact assessment** evaluates each identified aspect for its potential to cause environmental harm, considering normal operating conditions, abnormal conditions (start-up, shutdown, maintenance), and emergency situations.
- **Significance determination** uses a risk-based scoring matrix that considers the severity of the environmental impact, the likelihood of occurrence, the frequency of the activity, and the level of regulatory and stakeholder concern.
- **Significant aspects** are prioritized for operational controls, objective setting, and resource allocation.

The register of environmental aspects and impacts is maintained at the corporate level by the Environmental Compliance team and is reviewed and updated at least annually, or more frequently when there are material changes to operations, acquisitions, or regulatory requirements.

Current significant environmental aspects include:

1. Greenhouse gas emissions from energy consumption (electricity, natural gas, refrigerants).
2. Greenhouse gas emissions from the transportation and distribution fleet.
3. Generation of solid waste, including cardboard, wood, plastics, and construction materials.
4. Generation of hazardous waste, including paints, solvents, pesticides, and cleaning chemicals.
5. Water consumption in retail garden centers and landscape operations.
6. Storage and handling of chemicals and hazardous materials.
7. Stormwater runoff from distribution center and store parking lots.
8. Refrigerant leakage from commercial refrigeration systems.

#### 3.2.2 Compliance Obligations

BRHI maintains a comprehensive legal and other requirements register that identifies, at the federal, state, and local levels:

- All applicable environmental statutes, regulations, and ordinances.
- Permits, licenses, and registrations held by BRHI.
- Voluntary commitments, including ISO 14001 certification, Science Based Targets initiative (SBTi) commitments, CDP disclosures, and industry association pledges.
- Customer requirements related to environmental performance.
- Codes of practice and industry standards applicable to BRHI's operations.

The legal register is maintained by the Environmental Compliance team in partnership with the Legal Department and is reviewed quarterly for updates. A compliance gap analysis is performed annually to ensure all obligations are identified and addressed.

#### 3.2.3 Environmental Objectives and Targets

BRHI establishes measurable environmental objectives at the corporate, divisional, and facility levels. Objectives are:

- Consistent with the environmental policy.
- Considerate of significant environmental aspects, compliance obligations, and risks and opportunities.
- Monitored, communicated, and updated as appropriate.

Corporate-level environmental objectives and targets are detailed in the relevant sections of this policy. Divisional and facility-level objectives are documented in annual environmental action plans and are cascaded to relevant functions and levels within the organization.

Progress toward objectives is tracked through the quarterly environmental dashboard and reviewed during the annual management review.

### 3.3 Implementation and Operation

#### 3.3.1 Resources, Roles, Responsibilities, and Authority

BRHI ensures that the EMS is supported by adequate resources, including human resources, specialized skills, technology, and financial investment. Roles, responsibilities, and authorities for environmental management are defined as follows:

- **Board of Directors — Sustainability Committee:** Provides governance oversight of BRHI's environmental strategy, targets, and performance. Reviews annual sustainability report and management review outcomes.
- **Chief Executive Officer:** Bears ultimate accountability for BRHI's environmental performance. Approves this policy and the annual sustainability report.
- **Chief Operating Officer:** Ensures environmental considerations are integrated into operational planning, store operations, supply chain management, and capital expenditure decisions.
- **Vice President of Sustainability and Environmental Compliance:** Reports to the COO with a dotted-line reporting relationship to the Board Sustainability Committee. Responsible for the development, implementation, and continual improvement of the EMS. Leads the Sustainability and Environmental Compliance Department.
- **Director of Environmental Compliance:** Manages day-to-day regulatory compliance across all facilities. Oversees the environmental compliance audit program, permitting, and incident response.
- **Director of Sustainability Programs:** Leads climate strategy, energy management, waste reduction, water conservation, and sustainable products initiatives.
- **Regional Environmental Managers (6 positions):** Provide regional environmental compliance support, training, and oversight for stores and distribution centers within their assigned geographic areas.
- **Facility Environmental Coordinators:** Designated associates at each store and distribution center who serve as the local point of contact for environmental compliance, waste management, and sustainability programs.
- **All Associates:** Responsible for understanding and complying with the environmental procedures applicable to their roles, reporting environmental concerns, and participating in environmental training.

#### 3.3.2 Competence, Awareness, and Training

BRHI ensures that any person performing work on its behalf that has the potential to cause a significant environmental impact is competent on the basis of appropriate education, training, or experience. The environmental training program is detailed in Section 12 of this policy.

#### 3.3.3 Communication

BRHI communicates environmental information both internally and externally:

- **Internal communication:** Environmental performance updates are shared through the quarterly environmental dashboard (distributed to all management levels), the company intranet, associate newsletters, team huddle guides, and the BRHI Sustainability Portal.
- **External communication:** BRHI communicates its environmental commitments, performance, and progress through the annual Sustainability Report (GRI Standards-aligned), the corporate website, press releases, CDP disclosures, and direct engagement with investors, customers, and community stakeholders.
- **Grievance mechanism:** BRHI maintains an anonymous Environmental Concern Hotline (1-800-BRHI-ENVI) and an online reporting portal for associates, contractors, and community members to report environmental concerns without fear of retaliation.

#### 3.3.4 Documented Information

The EMS is supported by a hierarchy of documented information:

1. **Tier 1 — Policy:** This Environmental Sustainability and Compliance Policy.
2. **Tier 2 — Standards:** Corporate environmental standards and procedures (e.g., Hazardous Waste Management Standard, Spill Prevention Standard, Energy Management Standard).
3. **Tier 3 — Plans:** Facility-specific environmental plans, including spill prevention plans, stormwater pollution prevention plans, and waste management plans.
4. **Tier 4 — Records:** Environmental monitoring records, inspection logs, training records, audit reports, permits, and compliance documentation.

All EMS documentation is maintained in the BRHI Environmental Management System database (EMS-Online), which is the system of record for environmental compliance and performance data.

#### 3.3.5 Operational Planning and Control

BRHI establishes, implements, maintains, and controls the processes needed to meet environmental management system requirements and to implement actions determined during planning. This includes:

- **Standard Operating Procedures (SOPs):** Detailed procedures for all operations with significant environmental aspects, including chemical handling, waste management, fueling operations, and refrigerant management.
- **Maintenance programs:** Preventive maintenance schedules for pollution control equipment, refrigeration systems, HVAC systems, and fleet vehicles to minimize environmental releases.
- **Contractor management:** Environmental requirements incorporated into contractor agreements, including pre-qualification environmental screening, on-site environmental orientation, and performance monitoring.
- **Procurement controls:** Environmental criteria incorporated into procurement processes for products, services, and capital equipment.
- **Design controls:** Environmental impact assessments conducted for new store construction, major renovations, and significant operational changes.
- **Change management:** Management of change procedures that evaluate environmental impacts before implementing changes to processes, materials, equipment, or operations.

#### 3.3.6 Emergency Preparedness and Response

BRHI establishes, implements, and maintains procedures to prepare for and respond to potential emergency situations, including:

- Chemical spills and releases.
- Fires involving environmentally sensitive materials.
- Natural disasters affecting environmental controls.
- Refrigerant releases.
- Stormwater overflow events.

Emergency preparedness and response procedures are detailed in Section 11 of this policy and in facility-specific emergency response plans.

### 3.4 Checking and Corrective Action

#### 3.4.1 Monitoring, Measurement, Analysis, and Evaluation

BRHI monitors, measures, analyzes, and evaluates its environmental performance using the methods and metrics described in Section 13 of this policy. Monitoring activities include:

- Continuous energy monitoring through the enterprise Energy Management Information System (EMIS) at all major facilities.
- Monthly waste tracking and reporting through the Waste Management System.
- Quarterly water consumption tracking at all metered facilities.
- Refrigerant tracking through the Refrigerant Management System, including leak rate calculation per EPA Section 608 requirements.
- Continuous emissions monitoring at facilities with air quality permits.
- Semi-annual stormwater sampling at facilities subject to stormwater permits.
- Annual greenhouse gas inventory (Scope 1, 2, and 3) conducted in accordance with the GHG Protocol.

#### 3.4.2 Internal Audit

BRHI conducts a systematic, independent, and documented internal environmental audit program:

- **Audit scope:** All EMS elements, including corporate standards, facility-level compliance, and supply chain environmental requirements.
- **Audit frequency:** Each facility is audited at least once every 24 months. Facilities with higher environmental risk are audited annually. Corporate EMS elements are audited annually.
- **Audit team:** Audits are conducted by the Corporate Environmental Compliance Audit team, which operates independently of the functions being audited. Qualified third-party auditors supplement internal resources as needed.
- **Audit protocol:** Audit checklists are developed based on ISO 14001:2015 requirements, applicable regulations, and BRHI corporate environmental standards.
- **Findings classification:** Audit findings are classified as Major Nonconformity, Minor Nonconformity, Observation, or Opportunity for Improvement.
- **Corrective action:** All nonconformities require documented corrective action plans with defined owners and deadlines. Major nonconformities require immediate containment and root cause analysis. Corrective action completion is verified through follow-up audits.
- **Reporting:** Audit results are reported to facility management, regional environmental managers, the VP of Sustainability, and the Board Sustainability Committee.

#### 3.4.3 Evaluation of Compliance

BRHI evaluates fulfillment of its compliance obligations at least annually. The compliance evaluation:

- Assesses compliance with all applicable federal, state, and local environmental regulations.
- Reviews compliance with permit conditions and voluntary commitments.
- Identifies any gaps or areas of concern.
- Results in a documented compliance status report that is submitted to the VP of Sustainability and the Board Sustainability Committee.

#### 3.4.4 Nonconformity and Corrective Action

When a nonconformity occurs (including environmental incidents, regulatory violations, audit findings, or deviations from corporate standards), BRHI:

1. Reacts to the nonconformity and, as applicable, takes action to control and correct it, and deals with the consequences.
2. Evaluates the need for action to eliminate the causes of the nonconformity, in order that it does not recur or occur elsewhere, by reviewing the nonconformity, determining the causes, determining if similar nonconformities exist or could potentially occur.
3. Implements any action needed.
4. Reviews the effectiveness of any corrective action taken.
5. Makes changes to the EMS, if necessary.

All corrective actions are documented and tracked through the EMS-Online Corrective Action Tracking module.

### 3.5 Management Review

The top management of BRHI reviews the EMS at planned intervals to ensure its continuing suitability, adequacy, and effectiveness. The management review is conducted annually and includes consideration of:

- The status of actions from previous management reviews.
- Changes in external and internal issues relevant to the EMS, including the context of BRHI, the needs and expectations of interested parties, significant environmental aspects, risks and opportunities, and compliance obligations.
- The extent to which environmental objectives have been achieved.
- Information on BRHI's environmental performance, including trends in KPIs.
- The adequacy of resources.
- Relevant communication(s) from interested parties, including complaints.
- Opportunities for continual improvement.

Outputs of the management review include:

- Conclusions on the continuing suitability, adequacy, and effectiveness of the EMS.
- Decisions related to continual improvement opportunities.
- Decisions related to any need for changes to the EMS, including resources.
- Actions identified if environmental objectives have not been achieved.
- Opportunities to improve integration of the EMS with other business processes.
- Implications for BRHI's strategic direction.

The management review is chaired by the Chief Operating Officer and attended by the VP of Sustainability, Director of Environmental Compliance, Director of Sustainability Programs, Regional Environmental Managers, and representatives from Legal, Finance, Operations, Merchandising, and Supply Chain. Minutes and outputs of the management review are documented and maintained as EMS records. A summary is presented to the Board Sustainability Committee.

## 4. Climate and Energy

### 4.1 Greenhouse Gas Emissions Reduction Targets

BRHI has established science-based greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions reduction targets that are consistent with the level of decarbonization required to keep global temperature increase well below 2°C above pre-industrial levels, in alignment with the Paris Agreement. These targets have been submitted to and validated by the Science Based Targets initiative (SBTi).

#### 4.1.1 Scope 1 and Scope 2 Targets

- **Near-term target (by 2030, from a 2020 baseline):** Reduce absolute Scope 1 (direct) and Scope 2 (market-based indirect) greenhouse gas emissions by 50%.
- **Long-term target (by 2050):** Achieve net-zero Scope 1 and Scope 2 emissions.

Scope 1 emissions sources include: natural gas combustion for heating, diesel and gasoline consumption by the company-owned and operated fleet, refrigerant emissions from commercial refrigeration and HVAC systems, and emissions from on-site fuel combustion at distribution centers.

Scope 2 emissions sources include: purchased electricity consumed at all BRHI facilities.

#### 4.1.2 Scope 3 Target

- **Supply chain target (by 2035, from a 2020 baseline):** Reduce Scope 3 greenhouse gas emissions from purchased goods and services, upstream transportation, and the use of sold products by 30% per unit of revenue.

BRHI acknowledges that the majority of its carbon footprint resides in Scope 3 emissions, particularly in the manufacturing of products sold and the energy consumed by products during their useful life. BRHI will engage its top 200 suppliers (by GHG impact) to set their own science-based targets and to collaborate on product design improvements that reduce lifecycle emissions.

#### 4.1.3 Interim Milestones

| Year | Scope 1 & 2 Reduction | Renewable Electricity | Fleet Electrification |
|---|---|---|---|
| 2025 | 25% | 60% | 15% of last-mile fleet |
| 2027 | 35% | 80% | 35% of last-mile fleet |
| 2030 | 50% | 100% | 60% of last-mile fleet |
| 2035 | 70% | 100% (maintained) | 100% of last-mile fleet |
| 2040 | 85% | 100% (maintained) | 100% (maintained) |
| 2050 | Net-zero | 100% (maintained) | 100% (maintained) |

### 4.2 Energy Efficiency

BRHI is committed to reducing energy consumption across its operations through systematic efficiency improvements.

#### 4.2.1 Store Energy Efficiency

All BRHI retail stores and distribution centers shall implement the following energy efficiency measures:

- **LED Lighting:** All facility lighting has been converted to LED technology. New construction and major renovations shall use LED lighting exclusively, incorporating daylight harvesting controls in areas with sufficient natural light, occupancy sensors in stockrooms, restrooms, and low-traffic areas, and scheduled dimming during non-operating hours.
- **HVAC Optimization:** All HVAC systems shall be maintained and operated in accordance with ASHRAE standards. Facilities shall implement programmable thermostats with centralized control, variable frequency drives on HVAC motors and fans, economizer controls to maximize free cooling, demand-controlled ventilation based on occupancy, and regular HVAC system commissioning and recommissioning.
- **Energy Management Systems (EMS):** All stores and distribution centers with annual energy consumption exceeding 500,000 kWh shall be equipped with a computerized Energy Management System that provides real-time monitoring and control of lighting, HVAC, and refrigeration systems, automated scheduling and setpoint management, alarm management for equipment malfunctions and energy anomalies, integration with the enterprise Energy Management Information System (EMIS), and benchmarking against energy intensity targets (kWh per square foot).

#### 4.2.2 Distribution Center Energy Efficiency

Distribution centers shall additionally implement:

- High-efficiency dock door seals and loading dock controls.
- LED lighting with motion sensors in warehouse aisles.
- Variable speed drives on conveyor systems and material handling equipment.
- High-speed doors to minimize conditioned air loss.
- Reflective roof coatings to reduce cooling loads.

#### 4.2.3 Data Center and IT Energy Efficiency

BRHI's data centers and IT infrastructure shall pursue:

- Power Usage Effectiveness (PUE) target of 1.3 or lower.
- Server virtualization and consolidation.
- Hot aisle/cold aisle containment.
- Efficient uninterruptible power supply (UPS) systems.
- Responsible disposal and recycling of electronic equipment.

### 4.3 Renewable Energy

#### 4.3.1 100% Renewable Electricity Target

BRHI commits to sourcing 100% of the electricity consumed by its U.S. operations from renewable sources by 2030. This target will be achieved through a portfolio approach:

- **On-site solar installations:** BRHI will install rooftop and/or parking canopy solar photovoltaic (PV) systems at 500 or more stores and distribution centers by 2030. Each installation will be sized to maximize on-site generation while maintaining economic viability. Systems will be owned by BRHI or financed through third-party power purchase agreements (PPAs).
- **Off-site solar and wind power purchase agreements (PPAs):** BRHI will enter into long-term (10- to 20-year) virtual and physical PPAs for solar and wind energy to cover the portion of electricity consumption not met by on-site generation. PPAs will be structured to provide additionality, ensuring that BRHI's investment directly supports the development of new renewable generation capacity.
- **Green tariffs and utility programs:** Where PPAs are not feasible, BRHI will participate in utility green tariff programs that provide renewable electricity with credible attribution and additionality.
- **Renewable Energy Certificates (RECs):** As a transitional measure, BRHI may procure unbundled RECs to close any remaining gap between contracted renewable supply and total consumption. REC purchases will be from certified, verifiable sources (Green-e certified where available) and will be phased out as contracted renewable supply increases.

#### 4.3.2 On-site Solar Program

The on-site solar program targets 500+ installations by 2030 with the following specifications:

- **System size:** Average 250 kW to 1 MW per site, depending on roof/parking area availability and local grid interconnection capacity.
- **Total installed capacity target:** 300 MW by 2030.
- **Annual energy production target:** 450,000 MWh per year from on-site solar by 2030.
- **Preferred technologies:** Monocrystalline silicon PV panels with a minimum 25-year performance warranty, string inverters with 98.5%+ CEC efficiency, and monitoring systems integrated with the enterprise EMIS.
- **Decommissioning and recycling:** Solar panels removed during replacements or facility closures shall be recycled through certified e-waste recyclers.

#### 4.3.3 Battery Energy Storage

BRHI will evaluate and deploy battery energy storage systems (BESS) at select locations where technically and economically viable, to:

- Shift solar generation to peak demand periods.
- Provide demand charge management.
- Support grid resilience and emergency backup.

Target: 50 MWh of installed battery storage capacity by 2030.

### 4.4 Fleet Electrification

#### 4.4.1 Last-Mile Delivery Fleet

BRHI commits to transitioning its entire last-mile delivery fleet (estimated 3,500 vehicles) to battery electric vehicles (BEVs) by 2035. The transition plan includes:

- **Phase 1 (2024–2027):** Deploy 1,000 BEV delivery vans in metropolitan areas with established charging infrastructure. Install Level 2 and DC fast-charging stations at 150 distribution and fulfillment centers.
- **Phase 2 (2028–2030):** Deploy an additional 1,000 BEV delivery vans, expanding into suburban and secondary markets. Install charging infrastructure at 350 additional locations.
- **Phase 3 (2031–2035):** Complete transition of remaining fleet. Install charging infrastructure at all remaining locations.
- **Charging infrastructure:** All new distribution centers and major renovations shall include EV-ready electrical infrastructure (conduit, panel capacity, and conduit to parking areas). Charging stations shall be ENERGY STAR certified and capable of smart charging and load management.

#### 4.4.2 Employee and Customer Charging

BRHI will install EV charging stations for employee and customer use at select store locations:

- Target: Level 2 charging stations at 200 store locations by 2030.
- Priority given to locations in states with higher EV adoption rates and in areas with limited public charging infrastructure.
- Stations shall be networked, with usage data tracked for reporting purposes.

#### 4.4.3 Long-Haul and Regional Freight

For heavy-duty freight and line-haul operations (both company-owned and contracted):

- Pilot hydrogen fuel cell and battery electric Class 8 trucks as models become commercially available.
- Require contracted carriers to report fuel efficiency and emissions data.
- Set carrier sustainability requirements in all new and renewed transportation contracts, including idle reduction policies, EPA SmartWay participation, and fuel efficiency targets.

### 4.5 Energy Auditing

BRHI shall conduct energy audits at all top 100 energy-consuming sites (which collectively represent approximately 75% of total energy consumption) on an annual basis. Energy audits shall:

- Be conducted by Certified Energy Managers (CEMs) or equivalent qualified professionals.
- Follow ASHRAE Level II audit methodology at a minimum.
- Identify energy conservation measures (ECMs) with estimated costs, savings, and payback periods.
- Result in an Energy Action Plan for each audited facility, with approved projects entered into the capital expenditure pipeline.

Facilities not in the top 100 shall be audited at least once every three years using ASHRAE Level I methodology.

### 4.6 Green Building Standards

All new BRHI construction projects and major renovations (exceeding 50% of building replacement value) shall be designed and constructed to achieve LEED (Leadership in Energy and Environmental Design) certification at the Silver level or higher.

Specific green building requirements include:

- **Energy performance:** A minimum 20% improvement in energy performance over ASHRAE 90.1 baseline.
- **Water efficiency:** A minimum 30% reduction in indoor water use over EPA baseline.
- **Materials and resources:** A minimum of 20% of building materials (by cost) to contain recycled content, and a minimum of 50% of construction waste to be diverted from landfill.
- **Indoor environmental quality:** Compliance with ASHRAE 62.1 ventilation requirements and use of low-VOC materials.
- **Site sustainability:** Stormwater management controls, light pollution reduction, and heat island mitigation through reflective roofing and parking areas.

Major renovation projects that do not meet the threshold for full LEED certification shall still incorporate all applicable energy efficiency, water conservation, and sustainable material requirements.

## 5. Waste Management

### 5.1 Zero Waste to Landfill Goal

BRHI commits to achieving a 90% waste diversion rate from landfill and incineration (without energy recovery) across all operations by 2030, using a 2020 baseline. This goal is aligned with the industry-accepted definition of "zero waste to landfill" as a 90% or greater diversion rate.

The diversion rate shall be calculated as:

> **Diversion Rate (%) = (Total Waste Diverted / Total Waste Generated) × 100**

Where diverted waste includes materials recycled, composted, reused, or recovered through waste-to-energy processes (with energy recovery credited at 50% for diversion rate calculations).

#### 5.1.1 Interim Waste Diversion Milestones

| Year | Target Diversion Rate |
|---|---|
| 2023 | 65% |
| 2025 | 72% |
| 2027 | 80% |
| 2030 | 90% |

### 5.2 Recycling Programs

BRHI operates comprehensive recycling programs at all facilities. The following materials are recycled at every store and distribution center:

#### 5.2.1 Cardboard and Paper

- **Cardboard:** All corrugated cardboard from merchandise packaging, shipments, and operations shall be baled and recycled. Each facility shall maintain a baler and designated cardboard collection area. Target: 98% cardboard recovery rate.
- **Paper:** Office paper, newspapers, magazines, and mixed paper are collected through designated recycling containers. BRHI shall procure recycled-content paper (minimum 30% post-consumer recycled content) for all office and printing needs.

#### 5.2.2 Wood and Pallets

- **Wood pallets:** Reusable pallets are returned to vendors or pooled through BRHI's pallet management program. Damaged pallets are repaired on-site where feasible or recycled through wood recycling vendors.
- **Lumber and engineered wood:** Unsold or damaged lumber and engineered wood products are collected for recycling into mulch, biomass, or composite products. Treated wood is separated and managed in accordance with applicable regulations.

#### 5.2.3 Metals

- Steel, aluminum, copper, and other metals from operations, damaged merchandise, and fixture replacements are collected in designated metal recycling containers and sold to certified scrap metal recyclers.

#### 5.2.4 Plastics

- Stretch wrap, shrink wrap, and plastic banding from receiving operations are collected and baled for recycling.
- Plastic merchandise packaging (HDPE, PET, LDPE) is collected where recycling infrastructure exists.
- BRHI shall work with vendors to reduce plastic packaging and to transition to recyclable or compostable alternatives.

#### 5.2.5 Electronics

- Electronic waste (e-waste), including computers, monitors, printers, mobile devices, and point-of-sale equipment, is collected and recycled through R2 or e-Stewards certified recyclers.
- BRHI participates in and promotes consumer electronics take-back programs where state law requires or where voluntary programs are available.

#### 5.2.6 Batteries

- Lead-acid batteries (from automotive and tool rental operations) are collected and recycled through EPA-compliant battery recyclers.
- Rechargeable batteries (lithium-ion, NiMH, NiCd) are collected through the BRHI battery recycling program and recycled through certified processors.
- BRHI provides customer-facing battery collection receptacles at all store locations.

#### 5.2.7 Paint and Coatings

- BRHI operates a paint take-back and recycling program at all stores that sell paint. Collected paint is sorted, and usable paint is reprocessed into recycled-content paint. Non-usable paint is processed for proper disposal or energy recovery.
- Latex paint is consolidated and offered to community organizations where permitted by state regulations.
- Oil-based paint and solvents are managed as hazardous waste in accordance with Section 10.2 of this policy.

#### 5.2.8 Additional Materials

- Fluorescent lamps and ballasts (recycled through licensed mercury recycling facilities).
- Carpet and flooring remnants (recycled or donated where feasible).
- Appliances (refrigerant recovery and metal recycling).
- Compact fluorescent lamps (CFLs) and LED bulbs (collected for recycling).

### 5.3 Construction and Demolition Waste Diversion

All BRHI construction, renovation, and demolition projects shall implement construction and demolition (C&D) waste diversion plans with the following requirements:

- A minimum of 75% (by weight) of C&D waste shall be diverted from landfill.
- C&D waste management plans shall be developed for all projects exceeding $100,000 in scope and shall identify materials to be salvaged, recycled, or disposed of.
- Preferred C&D waste management vendors shall hold relevant state certifications and provide documented diversion reports.
- Reusable fixtures, equipment, and building materials from renovations shall be offered for reuse within BRHI or donated to qualified charitable organizations (e.g., Habitat for Humanity ReStore).

### 5.4 Food Waste Composting

At BRHI locations that include garden centers with living plant inventory:

- Organic waste (plant material, soil, and growing media) shall be composted on-site where local regulations permit, or collected for off-site composting.
- BRHI shall explore partnerships with local composting facilities and community gardens.
- Finished compost may be used in garden center operations or offered to customers and community organizations.
- Target: 80% diversion of organic waste from landfill at garden center locations by 2028.

### 5.5 Waste Auditing

BRHI conducts waste audits to measure, monitor, and improve waste management performance:

- **Quarterly waste audits** are conducted at all distribution centers and at a representative sample of at least 25 retail stores per quarter (rotated so that every store is audited at least once every two years).
- **Audit methodology:** Audits involve a visual assessment of waste streams, contamination rates, and diversion opportunities, supplemented by weigh-scale data from waste hauling and recycling vendors.
- **Audit reporting:** Waste audit results are documented and entered into the Waste Management System. Facility-level diversion rates are calculated and reported on the quarterly environmental dashboard.
- **Continuous improvement:** Facilities with diversion rates below target shall develop and implement corrective action plans within 30 days of audit completion.

### 5.6 Vendor Packaging Reduction

BRHI requires its vendors to participate in packaging reduction and sustainability efforts:

- **Sustainable Packaging Guidelines:** BRHI has published Sustainable Packaging Guidelines that apply to all products sold in BRHI stores. Guidelines specify preferred packaging materials (recyclable, recycled-content, or compostable), restrictions on excess packaging, and labeling requirements for recyclability.
- **Packaging reduction targets:** Top 100 vendors (by volume) shall reduce packaging materials (by weight per unit) by 15% by 2028 from a 2022 baseline, or demonstrate that packaging already meets BRHI's optimal packaging criteria.
- **Vendor scorecard:** Packaging sustainability is included in the vendor sustainability scorecard (see Section 8.6). Vendors that fail to meet packaging requirements may be subject to delisting.
- **Inbound shipping:** Vendors are required to use recyclable or returnable shipping materials where feasible. BRHI encourages the use of reusable totes and containers for inbound logistics.

## 6. Water Conservation

### 6.1 Water Reduction Target

BRHI commits to reducing total water withdrawal (from all sources) by 20% by 2030, measured against a 2020 baseline and normalized by revenue.

The water reduction target applies to all BRHI-owned and -operated facilities and includes potable water, reclaimed water, and well water used in operations. The target does not apply to water embedded in products sold.

#### 6.1.1 Interim Milestones

| Year | Target Reduction (vs. 2020 baseline, normalized) |
|---|---|
| 2025 | 8% |
| 2027 | 13% |
| 2030 | 20% |

### 6.2 Low-Flow Fixtures

All BRHI facilities shall use water-efficient fixtures:

- All restroom facilities shall be equipped with WaterSense-labeled (or equivalent) faucets, toilets, and urinals.
- New construction and renovations shall specify ultra-low-flow fixtures: faucets at 0.5 GPM, toilets at 1.28 GPF, urinals at 0.125 GPF (or waterless).
- Existing facilities shall be retrofitted with low-flow aerators and high-efficiency fixtures during scheduled renovations or within 36 months of policy effective date for priority facilities.
- Kitchen and break room faucets shall be equipped with low-flow aerators (1.5 GPM maximum).

### 6.3 Rainwater Harvesting

BRHI will install rainwater harvesting systems at new distribution center construction projects and at retail stores in water-stressed regions (as identified by the WRI Aqueduct Water Risk Atlas) where technically and economically feasible:

- Harvested rainwater shall be used for landscape irrigation, cooling tower makeup water (where applicable), and non-potable cleaning applications.
- Systems shall include first-flush diverters, filtration, and overflow protection.
- Target: Rainwater harvesting systems at 50 facilities by 2030.

### 6.4 Smart Irrigation

All BRHI garden centers with landscape irrigation systems shall be equipped with smart irrigation technology:

- Weather-based irrigation controllers (WBICs) or soil moisture sensor-based controllers.
- Drip irrigation for plant inventory areas.
- Rain sensors to prevent irrigation during and after precipitation events.
- Seasonal adjustment schedules to align with plant water requirements.
- Leak detection and automatic shut-off systems.

Garden center irrigation water use shall be sub-metered and tracked separately from other facility water use.

### 6.5 Water Quality Monitoring

BRHI implements water quality monitoring programs at facilities subject to water quality permits or located in sensitive watersheds:

- **Stormwater monitoring:** Facilities subject to stormwater discharge permits conduct sampling in accordance with permit requirements. Sampling results are reviewed quarterly and corrective actions are implemented for any exceedances.
- **Cooling tower water treatment:** Cooling towers (where present) are treated and monitored to maintain water quality, prevent legionella, and minimize blowdown water volume. Blowdown is managed in accordance with local discharge requirements.
- **Garden center runoff:** Garden centers capture and manage irrigation runoff to prevent nutrient and pesticide transport to stormwater systems. Best management practices include containment berms, vegetated filter strips, and directed drainage to sanitary sewer (where permitted).

## 7. Sustainable Products

### 7.1 Eco Options Program

BRHI operates the Eco Options program, which identifies, labels, and promotes products that offer demonstrable environmental benefits compared to conventional alternatives. Products bearing the Eco Options designation must meet one or more of the following criteria:

- **Energy Efficiency:** ENERGY STAR certified appliances, lighting, HVAC equipment, and building products; or products that exceed applicable federal energy efficiency standards by at least 10%.
- **Water Savings:** WaterSense certified plumbing fixtures, irrigation products, and appliances; or products that reduce water consumption by at least 20% compared to conventional alternatives.
- **Certified Wood and Paper:** Wood and paper products certified under the Forest Stewardship Council (FSC), Programme for the Endorsement of Forest Certification (PEFC), or Sustainable Forestry Initiative (SFI) chain of custody standards.
- **Low-VOC and Non-Toxic:** Paints, coatings, adhesives, sealants, and cleaning products that meet or exceed the South Coast Air Quality Management District (SCAQMD) Rule 1168 limits for VOCs, or are third-party certified under GREENGUARD, GREENGUARD Gold, EPA Safer Choice, or equivalent standards.
- **Recycled Content:** Products containing a minimum of 25% post-consumer recycled content or 40% pre-consumer recycled content, verified through third-party certification or manufacturer documentation.
- **Renewable Energy Products:** Solar panels, wind energy systems, battery storage, EV chargers, and related products that enable renewable energy generation or storage.
- **Durable and Reusable:** Products designed for extended life, repairability, and reuse, reducing the need for disposable alternatives.

The Eco Options program is managed by the Sustainability Programs team in coordination with the Merchandising department. Products are reviewed for Eco Options eligibility annually, and the program goals include increasing the Eco Options product assortment by 25% by 2028.

### 7.2 Chemical Management

#### 7.2.1 Restricted Substance List (RSL)

BRHI maintains a Restricted Substance List that identifies chemicals and substances that are prohibited or restricted in BRHI-owned brand products and in the products and packaging supplied to BRHI. The RSL is based on:

- U.S. federal and state regulatory requirements (including California Proposition 65, TSCA, and state chemical restrictions).
- European Union REACH Regulation Annex XVII and candidate list substances.
- International Agency for Research on Cancer (IARC) Group 1 and 2A carcinogens.
- Chemicals identified by the EPA Safer Choice program as chemicals of concern.
- Per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances (PFAS), formaldehyde, lead, mercury, cadmium, hexavalent chromium, and other substances of concern.

The RSL is maintained by the Product Safety and Compliance team and is updated at least annually. The current RSL is published on the BRHI vendor portal.

#### 7.2.2 Phase-Out Schedule

BRHI has established phase-out schedules for chemicals of concern found in products sold in its stores:

- **PFAS:** Eliminate PFAS from all BRHI-owned brand food contact materials, textiles, and packaging by the end of 2026. Eliminate PFAS from all remaining BRHI-owned brand products where technically feasible by the end of 2028.
- **Formaldehyde:** All BRHI-owned brand composite wood products shall comply with EPA TSCA Title VI (formaldehyde emission standards) by the effective dates of the regulation. BRHI encourages suppliers to achieve CARB Phase II or NAUF (No Added Urea Formaldehyde) standards.
- **Methylene chloride:** BRHI does not sell methylene chloride-based paint strikers in its stores. This ban has been in effect since 2019.
- **Neonicotinoids:** BRHI requires labeling on all neonicotinoid-containing lawn and garden products and is working with suppliers to develop and promote bee-friendly alternatives. Target: 50% reduction in neonicotinoid-containing product sales (by volume) by 2028.

#### 7.2.3 Chemical Inventory and SDS Management

BRHI maintains a comprehensive chemical inventory and Safety Data Sheet (SDS) management system:

- All chemicals present in BRHI facilities (including merchandise, cleaning products, and operational chemicals) are inventoried in the BRHI Chemical Management System (CMS).
- SDSs are maintained in both electronic and accessible formats at each facility for all hazardous chemicals present.
- The CMS is updated in real-time as new chemicals are introduced or formulations change.
- The CMS supports tier II reporting under EPCRA Section 312 and hazardous chemical inventory reporting under EPCRA Section 311.

### 7.3 Responsible Forestry

BRHI is committed to promoting responsible forest management and reducing the environmental impact of wood and paper products sold in its stores.

#### 7.3.1 Wood Purchasing Preferences

BRHI gives purchasing preference to wood and paper products certified under credible third-party forestry certification systems:

- **Second preference:** Programme for the Endorsement of Forest Certification (PEFC) certified products (including SFI and ATFS).

BRHI commits to eliminating wood and paper products sourced from deforestation or conversion of natural forests from its supply chain by 2028. This commitment applies to all BRHI-owned brand products and is a preferred expectation for all national brand products.

Vendors of wood and paper products shall:

- Provide chain-of-custody documentation tracing wood fiber to the forest of origin.

#### 7.3.3 Targets

- 100% of BRHI-owned brand wood products to be FSC or PEFC certified by 2028.
- 90% of all wood products sold in BRHI stores (by volume) to be certified or sourced from responsibly managed forests by 2030.

### 7.4 Conflict Minerals

BRHI is committed to ensuring that the products it sells do not contain conflict minerals (tin, tantalum, tungsten, and gold — commonly known as 3TG minerals) that directly or indirectly finance or benefit armed groups in the Democratic Republic of the Congo (DRC) or adjoining countries.

BRHI requires its suppliers of products containing 3TG minerals to:

- Conduct supply chain due diligence in accordance with the OECD Due Diligence Guidance for Responsible Supply Chains of Minerals from Conflict-Affected and High-Risk Areas.
- Provide completed Conflict Minerals Reporting Templates (CMRTs) upon request.
- Source 3TG minerals from smelters and refiners validated as conformant with the Responsible Minerals Initiative (RMI) Responsible Minerals Assurance Process (RMAP) or equivalent audit programs.

BRHI reports on its conflict minerals due diligence efforts in accordance with SEC Dodd-Frank Section 1502 requirements.

### 7.5 Product Transparency

BRHI encourages and, for BRHI-owned brands, requires product transparency through environmental product declarations (EPDs) and other disclosure mechanisms:

- **Environmental Product Declarations (EPDs):** All BRHI-owned brand products in the following categories shall have type III environmental product declarations (in accordance with ISO 14025) by 2030: insulation, flooring, paint and coatings, lumber and structural wood products, concrete and cement products, roofing materials, and doors and windows.
- **Health Product Declarations (HPDs):** All BRHI-owned brand interior building products shall have Health Product Declarations published by 2030.
- **Ingredient disclosure:** All BRHI-owned brand cleaning products shall disclose full ingredient lists on product packaging and on the BRHI website.

BRHI evaluates vendor sustainability performance through a Supply Chain Sustainability Scorecard. The scorecard is applied to all vendors supplying BRHI-owned brand products and is encouraged for all vendors. The scorecard assesses the following categories:

## 8. Pollution Prevention

### 8.1 Air Emissions Management

BRHI manages air emissions from its operations in compliance with the Clean Air Act and all applicable state and local air quality regulations.

#### 8.1.1 Stationary Source Emissions

- **Boilers and emergency generators:** BRHI maintains permits for all boilers and emergency generators as required by state and local air quality regulations. Equipment is operated and maintained to minimize emissions. All new boiler and generator purchases shall meet or exceed EPA emission standards.
- **Woodworking equipment:** Dust collection systems at distribution centers with woodworking operations are equipped with baghouse or cartridge filters to control particulate emissions. Filter performance is monitored through pressure differential gauges and opacity observations.
- **Paint mixing operations:** VOC emissions from paint tinting and mixing operations are managed through enclosed mixing systems and low-VOC tint formulations.

#### 8.1.2 Mobile Source Emissions

- Fleet electrification (see Section 4.4) is the primary strategy for reducing mobile source emissions.
- During the transition period, BRHI requires all fleet vehicles to comply with applicable EPA emission standards and state anti-idling regulations.
- Diesel vehicles are equipped with diesel particulate filters and selective catalytic reduction systems where required by regulation.

### 8.2 Spill Prevention and Response

BRHI maintains Spill Prevention, Control, and Countermeasure (SPCC) plans at all facilities where oil storage exceeds the threshold quantities specified in 40 CFR Part 112.

#### 8.2.1 SPCC Plan Requirements

SPCC plans are maintained and implemented at:

- All distribution centers with aboveground oil storage capacity exceeding 1,320 gallons (aggregate) or underground storage exceeding 42,000 gallons.
- All retail stores with fuel storage for fleet or equipment operations exceeding applicable thresholds.

SPCC plans include:

- Facility diagram showing all oil storage locations, containment structures, and drainage patterns.
- Spill prediction analysis identifying potential spill volumes, flow paths, and receptors.
- Secondary containment requirements for all bulk oil storage (110% of the largest container or 10% of aggregate capacity, whichever is greater).
- Operating procedures for oil delivery, storage, and transfer to prevent spills.
- Spill response procedures and equipment inventories (spill kits at all oil storage and handling locations).
- Emergency contact information for internal response teams and regulatory agencies.
- Inspection and testing schedules for tanks, piping, and containment structures.
- Training requirements for associates handling oil.

SPCC plans are reviewed and recertified by a licensed Professional Engineer at least every five years, or whenever there is a material change in facility oil storage operations.

#### 8.2.2 Chemical Spill Prevention

For non-petroleum chemicals (pesticides, cleaning products, solvents, paints, etc.):

- All chemicals are stored in designated areas with appropriate secondary containment, ventilation, and segregation from incompatible materials.
- Chemical storage areas are inspected weekly by the Facility Environmental Coordinator.
- Spill kits appropriate to the types of chemicals stored are maintained at each facility, with contents inspected monthly and replenished as needed.
- All chemical deliveries are supervised by a trained associate.

### 8.3 Stormwater Management

BRHI manages stormwater at its facilities in compliance with the Clean Water Act and all applicable state and local stormwater regulations.

#### 8.3.1 Stormwater Pollution Prevention Plans (SWPPPs)

SWPPPs are maintained at all BRHI facilities required to obtain stormwater discharge permits (including construction permits and industrial activity permits). SWPPPs include:

- Site maps showing drainage areas, outfalls, structural controls, and potential pollutant sources.
- Description of potential pollutant sources specific to the facility.
- Best Management Practices (BMPs) to prevent stormwater contamination, including: good housekeeping practices (parking lot sweeping, dumpster management, material storage), structural controls (retention ponds, bioswales, oil/water separators, vegetated filter strips), and seasonal BMP maintenance schedules.
- Quarterly visual monitoring of stormwater discharge at all outfalls.
- Benchmark monitoring as required by the applicable multi-sector general permit.
- Annual SWPPP review and update.

#### 8.3.2 Construction Stormwater Management

All BRHI construction projects that disturb one acre or more of land shall:

- Obtain coverage under the applicable Construction General Permit.
- Develop and implement a site-specific SWPPP.
- Install and maintain erosion and sediment controls in accordance with the SWPPP.
- Conduct inspections at the frequency required by the permit (typically every 7 days or within 24 hours of a 0.5-inch or greater rainfall event).
- Maintain inspection logs and corrective action documentation.

### 8.4 Refrigerant Management

BRHI manages refrigerants in all commercial refrigeration and HVAC systems to minimize releases and ensure compliance with EPA regulations under Section 608 and Section 609 of the Clean Air Act, as amended by the AIM Act.

#### 8.4.1 Refrigerant Transition

BRHI commits to transitioning from high-global warming potential (GWP) refrigerants to low-GWP alternatives:

- **Target:** Reduce refrigerant-related GHG emissions (Scope 1) by 40% by 2030 from a 2020 baseline.
- **New installations:** All new commercial refrigeration systems installed after January 1, 2026, shall use refrigerants with a GWP of 150 or less (e.g., CO2 [R-744], propane [R-290], or HFO blends), where commercially available and code-permitted.
- **Existing systems:** Existing systems using high-GWP refrigerants (e.g., R-404A, R-410A) shall be evaluated for retrofit or replacement during scheduled system end-of-life or major maintenance events. Transition plans shall be developed for the top 100 highest-emitting systems.
- **Leak detection:** All commercial refrigeration systems with a charge exceeding 50 pounds shall be equipped with automated refrigerant leak detection systems that continuously monitor for leaks and generate alerts. Systems with a charge exceeding 500 pounds shall have leak detection integrated with the facility EMS for real-time monitoring.

#### 8.4.2 Refrigerant Tracking and Reporting

- All refrigerant purchases, additions, recoveries, and disposals are tracked in the Refrigerant Management System.
- Leak rates are calculated quarterly for each system in accordance with EPA Section 608 requirements.
- Systems exceeding applicable EPA leak rate thresholds (currently 20% for commercial refrigeration, 10% for commercial AC) are repaired within the required timeframe (30 days) or, if repair is not feasible, a replacement plan is developed and implemented.
- Annual refrigerant emissions are reported as part of BRHI's Scope 1 GHG inventory.

### 8.5 Noise Pollution Mitigation

BRHI recognizes that its distribution centers and delivery fleet operations can generate noise that affects surrounding communities.

- **Distribution centers:** All new distribution center construction projects shall include noise impact assessments. Noise mitigation measures shall include: berms or sound walls where operations are adjacent to residential areas, restricted hours for outdoor operations involving amplified noise, and muffler requirements on all material handling equipment.
- **Delivery fleet:** BRHI shall specify noise-reduction features on delivery vehicles, including low-noise refrigeration units on refrigerated trucks. Delivery schedules in residential areas shall respect local noise ordinances.
- **Complaint response:** All noise complaints from community members are logged, investigated, and addressed within 14 business days. Recurring noise issues are escalated to the Regional Environmental Manager for resolution.

## 9. Regulatory Compliance

### 9.1 Federal Environmental Regulations

BRHI complies with all applicable federal environmental regulations, including but not limited to:

#### 9.1.1 Resource Conservation and Recovery Act (RCRA)

BRHI manages hazardous waste in strict compliance with RCRA Subtitle C requirements:

- **Generator status:** BRHI facilities operate as either conditionally exempt small quantity generators (CESQGs), small quantity generators (SQGs), or large quantity generators (LQGs) under RCRA, depending on the volume and type of hazardous waste generated. Generator status is determined at each facility and is reviewed annually.
- **Hazardous waste identification:** All waste streams at BRHI facilities are evaluated for hazardous waste characteristics (ignitability, corrosivity, reactivity, toxicity) and for listed waste designations. Hazardous waste determinations are documented and maintained on file.
- **Storage and labeling:** Hazardous waste is stored in designated, secure accumulation areas with appropriate secondary containment, ventilation, and signage. Containers are labeled with the words "Hazardous Waste," the applicable EPA waste code(s), and the accumulation start date. Satellite accumulation areas are managed in accordance with 40 CFR 262.15.
- **Manifesting and disposal:** All hazardous waste shipments are accompanied by a Uniform Hazardous Waste Manifest. BRHI uses only EPA-permitted treatment, storage, and disposal facilities (TSDFs). Manifest copies are maintained for a minimum of three years and are reviewed for completeness and accuracy.
- **Universal waste:** BRHI manages universal wastes (batteries, pesticides, mercury-containing equipment, and lamps) under the streamlined universal waste regulations at 40 CFR Part 273.
- **Aerosol cans:** BRHI manages waste aerosol cans as universal waste where state regulations permit.
- **Pharmaceutical waste:** BRHI does not operate pharmacies but manages any pharmaceutical-type products (e.g., over-the-counter medications sold in some stores) in accordance with applicable regulations.
- **Land disposal restrictions:** All hazardous waste is treated to meet land disposal restriction (LDR) standards before land disposal. LDR notifications are included with each manifest.

#### 9.1.2 Clean Air Act (CAA)

BRHI complies with all applicable Clean Air Act requirements:

- **Title V operating permits:** Maintained at facilities where applicable (e.g., distribution centers with large boilers).
- **New Source Performance Standards (NSPS):** Compliance with applicable NSPS for new and modified emission units.
- **National Emission Standards for Hazardous Air Pollutants (NESHAP):** Compliance with applicable NESHAP, including those for boilers, reciprocating internal combustion engines (RICE), and halogenated solvent cleaning.
- **Section 608 refrigerant management:** Compliance with refrigerant venting prohibitions, technician certification requirements, refrigerant recovery and recycling, leak repair requirements, and recordkeeping and reporting requirements. As amended by the AIM Act, compliance with HFC phasedown requirements.
- **State Implementation Plans (SIPs):** Compliance with all applicable SIP requirements, including VOC content limits for paints and coatings.

#### 9.1.3 Clean Water Act (CWA)

BRHI complies with all applicable Clean Water Act requirements:

- **National Pollutant Discharge Elimination System (NPDES) permits:** Maintained at facilities with stormwater discharges associated with industrial activity and at distribution centers with process wastewater discharges.
- **Stormwater permits:** Coverage under the applicable multi-sector general permit (MSGP) for industrial activities and construction general permit (CGP) for construction activities.
- **Pretreatment requirements:** Facilities discharging to publicly owned treatment works (POTWs) comply with applicable pretreatment standards and local sewer use ordinances.
- **Spill prevention:** SPCC plans maintained as described in Section 8.2.

#### 9.1.4 Toxic Substances Control Act (TSCA)

BRHI complies with TSCA requirements as they apply to its operations:

- **Lead-based paint:** All BRHI associates involved in paint sales or providing advice on renovation activities are trained in the EPA Renovation, Repair, and Painting (RRP) Rule requirements. BRHI sells only lead-free paint.
- **TSCA Title VI (formaldehyde):** All composite wood products sold by BRHI comply with the formaldehyde emission standards under TSCA Title VI and the California Air Resources Board (CARB) Airborne Toxic Control Measure (ATCM) Phase II.
- **PFAS reporting:** BRHI complies with TSCA Section 8(a)(7) reporting requirements for PFAS as applicable.
- **Asbestos:** BRHI maintains asbestos management plans at older facilities where asbestos-containing materials (ACMs) have been identified. ACMs are managed in place, encapsulated, or removed by licensed asbestos abatement contractors in accordance with NESHAP requirements.

#### 9.1.5 Emergency Planning and Community Right-to-Know Act (EPCRA)

BRHI complies with EPCRA requirements at all applicable facilities:

- **Section 302 — Emergency Planning Notification:** Notification to the State Emergency Response Commission (SERC) and Local Emergency Planning Committee (LEPC) for facilities holding Extremely Hazardous Substances (EHSs) above threshold planning quantities.

Annual submission of Tier II (or state equivalent) chemical inventory reports by March 1 of each year.

## 10. Environmental Incident Response

### 10.1 Spill Response Procedures

BRHI maintains detailed spill response procedures for all facilities. These procedures are documented in facility-specific emergency response plans and the BRHI Spill Response Standard Operating Procedure (SOP-ENV-012).

#### 10.1.1 Spill Classification

| Classification | Definition | Response Level |
|---|---|---|
| **Minor** | Less than 1 gallon of non-hazardous material; contained within secondary containment or impervious surface | Facility-level response; Facility Environmental Coordinator manages |
| **Moderate** | 1–10 gallons of hazardous material or any quantity reaching a drain or waterway | Facility-level response with Regional Environmental Manager notification |
| **Major** | More than 10 gallons of hazardous material; any release to the environment (soil, water, air); any release exceeding a regulatory reportable quantity | Immediate escalation to Regional Environmental Manager, Director of Environmental Compliance, and VP of Sustainability. External spill response contractor activated as needed |

#### 10.1.2 Response Procedures

For all spills:

1. **Safety first:** Ensure the safety of associates and customers. Evacuate the immediate area if there is a risk of fire, explosion, or toxic exposure. Do not attempt to clean up a spill unless trained and equipped to do so.
2. **Stop the source:** If it can be done safely, stop the source of the spill (e.g., close a valve, upright a container, place a leaking container in overpack).
3. **Contain the spill:** Use absorbent materials, booms, or dikes from the nearest spill kit to contain the spill and prevent it from reaching drains, waterways, or permeable surfaces.
4. **Notify:** Report the spill immediately to the Facility Environmental Coordinator and store/distribution center manager. For moderate and major spills, activate the notification chain described in Section 10.2.
5. **Clean up:** For minor spills, trained associates may clean up using spill kit materials. For moderate and major spills, or spills involving hazardous materials, cleanup shall be performed by trained personnel or a contracted spill response company.
6. **Dispose:** All spill cleanup materials shall be managed as hazardous waste unless determined to be non-hazardous through proper waste characterization.
7. **Document:** Complete a Spill Incident Report (Form ENV-045) within 24 hours. Include photographs, estimated volume, materials involved, response actions taken, and root cause (if known).

#### 10.1.3 Spill Response Equipment

Spill kits are maintained at the following locations at every BRHI facility:

- Fuel storage and dispensing areas.
- Chemical storage areas.
- Loading docks.
- Garden center chemical storage (where applicable).
- Paint department (where applicable).
- Tool rental maintenance areas (where applicable).

Spill kit contents are inspected monthly by the Facility Environmental Coordinator and replenished as needed. Spill kits include absorbent pads and socks, absorbent granular material, nitrile gloves, safety goggles, disposal bags, and a laminated quick-reference card with emergency contact numbers.

### 10.2 Reporting Requirements

#### 10.2.1 Internal Reporting

All environmental incidents shall be reported internally as follows:

- **Immediate (within 1 hour):** All spills classified as moderate or major, all releases to the environment, all regulatory citations or notices of violation, and all situations that may result in regulatory reporting shall be reported to the Regional Environmental Manager and the Director of Environmental Compliance via the BRHI Environmental Incident Hotline (1-800-BRHI-ENVI) and the EMS-Online Incident Reporting module.
- **Within 24 hours:** All spills classified as minor shall be reported through EMS-Online. All environmental incidents (including near-misses) shall be documented on Form ENV-045.
- **Within 48 hours:** The Director of Environmental Compliance shall provide an initial incident summary to the VP of Sustainability for all moderate and major incidents.

#### 10.2.2 Regulatory Reporting

Regulatory notifications and reports shall be made in accordance with applicable federal, state, and local requirements, including but not limited to:

- **National Response Center (NRC):** Immediate notification for releases of CERCLA hazardous substances or EPCRA EHSs above reportable quantities (1-800-424-8802).
- **EPCRA notifications:** Immediate notification to the SERC, LEPC, and local fire department for releases of EHSs above reportable quantities (Section 304).
- **State environmental agencies:** Notification within timeframes specified by state regulations (typically 24 hours or less for significant releases).
- **Follow-up written reports:** Submitted to applicable regulatory agencies within the timeframes specified in permits or regulations (typically 5 to 15 days).

The Director of Environmental Compliance or their designee is responsible for making all regulatory notifications. Regulatory reporting shall not be delayed pending completion of the internal investigation. Legal Counsel shall be consulted before submitting any written regulatory reports that may have legal implications.

### 10.3 Remediation Process

When an environmental release results in soil, groundwater, or surface water contamination, BRHI will implement a remediation process:

1. **Immediate response:** Contain the release and prevent further migration of contamination. Engage a qualified environmental consulting firm to assess the extent of contamination.
2. **Site characterization:** Conduct a Phase I Environmental Site Assessment (if not previously performed) and Phase II investigation to define the nature and extent of contamination.
3. **Remediation planning:** Develop a remediation action plan in consultation with the environmental consultant, Legal Counsel, and the applicable regulatory agency. The plan shall identify remediation goals, technologies, schedule, and cost estimates.
4. **Remediation implementation:** Execute the remediation action plan using qualified contractors. Conduct ongoing monitoring to track remediation progress.
5. **Closure:** Obtain regulatory closure (No Further Action letter or equivalent) upon successful completion of remediation. Maintain documentation of the remediation in the facility environmental file.

BRHI maintains financial assurance for environmental remediation through its corporate environmental liability insurance program and environmental reserves.

### 10.4 Root Cause Analysis

For all moderate and major environmental incidents, BRHI conducts a formal root cause analysis (RCA) within 30 days of the incident. The RCA:

- Uses a structured methodology (e.g., 5 Whys, fishbone diagram, or fault tree analysis).
- Identifies the root cause(s) and contributing factors of the incident.
- Determines whether the incident resulted from equipment failure, human error, procedural deficiency, training gap, or systemic issue.
- Develops corrective actions to address root causes and prevent recurrence.
- Assigns corrective action owners and deadlines.
- Is documented and maintained in EMS-Online.

Corrective actions resulting from RCAs are tracked to completion and verified for effectiveness. Lessons learned from RCAs are communicated to all facilities through the quarterly environmental newsletter and incorporated into training materials and standard operating procedures as appropriate.

## 11. Environmental Training

### 11.1 Training Program Overview

BRHI maintains a comprehensive environmental training program to ensure that all associates understand their environmental responsibilities and have the knowledge and skills necessary to perform their duties in an environmentally responsible manner. Training is managed through the BRHI Learning Management System (LMS) and is tracked and documented as part of the EMS.

### 11.2 New Hire Orientation

All new BRHI associates (full-time, part-time, and seasonal) shall complete the Environmental Awareness module as part of the new hire orientation process within their first 14 days of employment. The module covers:

- BRHI's environmental policy and commitments.
- The role of every associate in environmental stewardship.
- Facility-specific environmental procedures, including waste management (trash, recycling, hazardous waste), chemical handling basics, spill reporting, and energy and water conservation.
- The Environmental Concern Hotline and how to report environmental issues.
- Key environmental compliance requirements relevant to the associate's role.

Completion of this module is tracked in the LMS and is a prerequisite for the completion of the new hire probationary period.

### 11.3 Annual Refresher Training

All associates who handle, manage, or come into contact with chemicals or hazardous materials as part of their regular duties shall complete annual refresher training. This includes, but is not limited to, associates in the following roles:

- Paint department associates.
- Garden center associates.
- Tool rental maintenance associates.
- Receiving and stockroom associates.
- Facilities maintenance associates.
- Pro desk associates handling bulk chemical sales.
- Delivery drivers transporting chemicals.

Annual refresher training covers:

- Hazardous waste management procedures (RCRA requirements, satellite accumulation, labeling, manifesting).
- Chemical storage and handling procedures (SDS access, personal protective equipment, incompatible materials).
- Spill response procedures (classification, containment, reporting).
- Refrigerant management awareness (for facilities maintenance associates).
- Updates to environmental regulations and BRHI environmental standards.
- Review of environmental incidents and lessons learned from the preceding year.

Annual refresher training must be completed by March 31 of each calendar year. Completion is tracked in the LMS, and completion rates are reported on the quarterly environmental dashboard. Target: 100% completion.

### 11.4 Specialized Training

BRHI provides specialized environmental training for associates in designated environmental compliance and management roles:

#### 11.4.1 Facility Environmental Coordinators

Facility Environmental Coordinators shall complete:

- **Initial training (within 90 days of assignment):** A 16-hour Facility Environmental Coordinator Training Program covering RCRA hazardous waste management, CAA refrigerant management, stormwater management, SPCC plan implementation, chemical inventory management, environmental recordkeeping, and EMS-Online system training.
- **Annual refresher:** An 8-hour annual refresher course covering regulatory updates, new BRHI environmental standards, incident case studies, and best practice sharing.
- **RCRA training:** Training that meets the requirements of 40 CFR 265.16 for LQG facilities or 40 CFR 262.34(d)(5)(iii) for SQG facilities, as applicable.

#### 11.4.2 Refrigerant Handling

All associates who service, maintain, or repair commercial refrigeration or HVAC systems shall hold current EPA Section 608 Technician Certification (Universal or applicable type). BRHI provides:

- Study materials and examination proctoring for EPA Section 608 certification.
- Annual training on refrigerant management procedures, leak detection technology, and low-GWP refrigerant handling.
- Training on the AIM Act HFC phasedown schedule and compliance requirements.

#### 11.4.3 Hazardous Waste Handlers

Associates who sign hazardous waste manifests or make hazardous waste determinations shall receive training in:

- RCRA hazardous waste identification and classification.
- Proper container management, labeling, and accumulation requirements.
- Manifest preparation and review.
- Land disposal restrictions (LDR) notifications.
- Contingency plan implementation (for LQG facilities).
- DOT hazardous materials shipping requirements (49 CFR Parts 171–180), as applicable.

#### 11.4.4 Stormwater Management

Associates responsible for stormwater inspections, sampling, or SWPPP implementation shall receive training in:

- SWPPP requirements and best management practices.
- Visual monitoring and sampling procedures.
- Inspection documentation and reporting.
- Quarterly stormwater training refreshers, with additional training triggered by any benchmark exceedance.

#### 11.4.5 SPCC Plan Implementation

Associates at facilities with SPCC plans who handle oil in quantities that could cause a discharge shall receive training in:

- Oil handling procedures to prevent spills.
- SPCC plan requirements specific to their facility.
- Spill response procedures and equipment deployment.
- Annual SPCC plan review and training as required by 40 CFR 112.7(e)(10).

### 11.5 Training Records

All environmental training records are maintained in the BRHI LMS and include:

- Associate name and employee ID.
- Training course title and description.
- Date of training.
- Training provider (internal or external).
- Assessment results (where applicable).
- Certification expiration dates (where applicable).

Training records are maintained for the duration of employment plus three years, or longer if required by regulation.

### 12.2 Annual Sustainability Report

BRHI publishes an annual Sustainability Report that is publicly available on the BRHI corporate website. The report is prepared in accordance with the Global Reporting Initiative (GRI) Standards (Comprehensive option) and includes:

- CEO sustainability message.
- Materiality assessment results.
- Environmental performance data and trend analysis.
- Progress toward all public environmental targets.
- Case studies and highlight stories.
- GRI Content Index.
- SASB (Sustainability Accounting Standards Board) metrics for the retail and home building industries.

The annual Sustainability Report is published by June 30 of each year, covering the preceding calendar year. The report is prepared by the Sustainability Programs team, reviewed by the VP of Sustainability and the COO, and approved by the CEO and the Board Sustainability Committee. The report is independently verified by a qualified third-party assurance provider (limited assurance level, progressing to reasonable assurance by 2028).

### 12.3 CDP Disclosure

BRHI submits annual disclosures to the Carbon Disclosure Project (CDP) in the following areas:

- **CDP Climate Change:** Comprehensive disclosure of climate governance, strategy, risk management, metrics, and targets. BRHI targets an A- or higher CDP Climate score by 2028.
- **CDP Water Security:** Disclosure of water governance, risks, opportunities, and management approaches.
- **CDP Forests:** Disclosure of deforestation risks and responsible forestry practices, with a focus on timber and palm oil commodities.

CDP responses are submitted annually by the deadline established by CDP (typically July).

### 12.4 TCFD-Aligned Climate Risk Disclosure

BRHI discloses climate-related risks and opportunities in alignment with the recommendations of the Task Force on Climate-related Financial Disclosures (TCFD). Disclosure includes:

#### 12.4.1 Governance

- Board Sustainability Committee oversight of climate-related risks and opportunities.
- Management's role in assessing and managing climate risks, including the VP of Sustainability's reporting structure.

#### 12.4.2 Strategy

- Identification of climate-related risks and opportunities over the short (0–3 years), medium (3–10 years), and long (10+ years) term.
- Scenario analysis using a 1.5°C scenario and a 3°C+ scenario to assess resilience of BRHI's strategy.
- Transition risks: policy and legal, technology, market, and reputation.
- Physical risks: acute (extreme weather events) and chronic (temperature changes, water stress, sea level rise).

#### 12.4.3 Risk Management

- Processes for identifying, assessing, and managing climate-related risks.
- Integration of climate risk into BRHI's enterprise risk management (ERM) framework.

#### 12.4.4 Metrics and Targets

- Scope 1, 2, and 3 GHG emissions.
- Science-based targets and progress.
- Climate-related metrics, including energy consumption, renewable energy percentage, and fleet electrification progress.
- Internal carbon price: BRHI applies a $50/metric ton CO2e internal carbon price to major capital expenditure decisions (escalating to $75 by 2028).

TCFD-aligned disclosures are included in the annual Sustainability Report and are referenced in BRHI's annual financial filings (10-K report).

### 12.5 Quarterly Internal Environmental Dashboard

BRHI maintains a quarterly internal environmental dashboard that provides real-time and near-real-time environmental performance data to management at all levels. The dashboard includes:

- Energy consumption and intensity by facility, region, and company total.
- GHG emissions estimates (monthly, based on activity data).
- Waste generation and diversion rates by facility, region, and company total.
- Water consumption by facility, region, and company total.
- Refrigerant leak rates by facility.
- Environmental incident counts and trends.
- Corrective action status.
- Training completion rates.
- Regulatory compliance status.

The dashboard is accessible through the EMS-Online system and is distributed to facility managers, regional environmental managers, and senior leadership within 30 days of the end of each quarter.

## 13. Stakeholder Engagement

### 13.1 Customer Education

BRHI recognizes that the environmental impact of the products it sells extends far beyond its own operations — the majority of the environmental footprint of home improvement products occurs during their use in customers' homes. BRHI is committed to helping customers make more sustainable choices:

- **Eco Options labeling:** The Eco Options label (see Section 7.1) provides a clear, recognizable signal to customers that a product offers environmental benefits. Eco Options products are highlighted in store displays, online product pages, and advertising circulars.
- **In-store sustainability education:** BRHI will develop and deploy in-store sustainability education materials, including signage near relevant product categories, QR codes linking to sustainability product information on the BRHI website, and informational brochures on topics such as home energy efficiency, water conservation, and sustainable landscaping.
- **Workshops and events:** BRHI stores will host a minimum of two sustainability-focused DIY workshops per year, covering topics such as home energy audits, solar panel installation, water-efficient landscaping, and low-VOC painting.
- **Online resources:** The BRHI website will maintain a dedicated sustainability section that includes product sustainability information, home energy and water savings calculators, guidance on sustainable home improvement projects, and links to BRHI's sustainability report and environmental commitments.
- **Product ratings and reviews:** BRHI will integrate sustainability attributes into its online product search and filtering tools, allowing customers to filter products by environmental criteria (ENERGY STAR, WaterSense, FSC certified, low-VOC, recycled content).

### 13.2 Supplier Sustainability Partnerships

BRHI engages its suppliers as partners in achieving its sustainability goals:

- **Top 200 supplier engagement:** BRHI will engage its top 200 suppliers (by Scope 3 emissions impact) to set their own science-based emissions reduction targets. BRHI will provide technical assistance and share best practices through the annual Supplier Sustainability Summit.
- **Joint innovation:** BRHI will collaborate with key suppliers on sustainable product development, including low-carbon materials, circular product design, and reduced-impact packaging.
- **Supplier scorecard:** The Supply Chain Sustainability Scorecard (see Section 7.6) creates transparency and accountability in supplier environmental performance.
- **Capacity building:** BRHI will offer sustainability training and resources to small and medium-sized suppliers through the BRHI Supplier Sustainability Academy, a web-based training platform covering energy management, waste reduction, chemical management, and responsible sourcing.
- **Sustainable sourcing committees:** BRHI merchandising teams will include sustainability criteria in all category reviews and new vendor evaluations.

### 13.3 Community Environmental Initiatives

BRHI invests in the environmental health of the communities where it operates:

- **Community tree planting:** BRHI will plant 500,000 trees in communities across the United States by 2030 through partnerships with the Arbor Day Foundation and local organizations.
- **Park and green space improvement:** BRHI will donate materials and volunteer labor for 100 park and green space improvement projects per year through the BRHI Foundation.
- **Habitat for Humanity partnership:** BRHI will continue its partnership with Habitat for Humanity, providing discounted sustainable building materials and associate volunteer labor for energy-efficient affordable housing construction.
- **Water stewardship:** BRHI will support community water conservation and water quality improvement projects in water-stressed regions where it operates, with a target of $2 million in annual community water stewardship investments by 2028.
- **Environmental education grants:** BRHI will provide grants of up to $5,000 to K-12 schools for environmental education projects, with a target of 200 grants per year.
- **Disaster response:** BRHI will maintain a rapid response capability to provide materials and support for community environmental recovery following natural disasters.

### 13.4 Industry Collaboration

BRHI collaborates with industry peers, associations, and multi-stakeholder initiatives to advance environmental sustainability across the home improvement and retail sectors:

- **Retail Industry Leaders Association (RILA):** Active participation in RILA's sustainability committee and sharing of best practices.
- **Home Improvement Research Institute (HIRI):** Funding and participating in research on sustainable home improvement trends and consumer behavior.
- **Science Based Targets initiative (SBTi):** Commitment to and validation of science-based emissions reduction targets.
- **We Mean Business Coalition:** Participation in climate advocacy initiatives.
- **Responsible Business Alliance (RBA):** Membership and adoption of responsible sourcing standards.
- **Forest Stewardship Council (FSC):** Membership and promotion of FSC-certified products.
- **U.S. Green Building Council (USGBC):** Membership and advocacy for green building practices.
- **EPA SmartWay:** Partnership for freight sustainability benchmarking and improvement.

## 14. Environmental Governance

### 14.1 Board of Directors — Sustainability Committee

The BRHI Board of Directors has established a standing Sustainability Committee composed of no fewer than three independent directors. The Sustainability Committee's charter includes oversight of the following environmental matters:

- Review and approval of BRHI's environmental strategy, policy, and public commitments.
- Oversight of environmental performance, including progress toward science-based targets and other environmental goals.
- Review of the annual Sustainability Report and TCFD-aligned disclosures.
- Review of environmental risks and their integration into BRHI's enterprise risk management framework.
- Oversight of environmental regulatory compliance, including any material regulatory actions, fines, or enforcement proceedings.
- Review of environmental incident reports for moderate and major incidents.
- Evaluation of the adequacy of environmental governance structures, resources, and staffing.
- Engagement with external stakeholders on environmental matters, as appropriate.

The Sustainability Committee meets at least quarterly and reports to the full Board of Directors after each meeting. Meeting minutes are maintained as corporate records.

### 14.2 Vice President of Sustainability and Environmental Compliance

The VP of Sustainability and Environmental Compliance is the senior executive responsible for BRHI's environmental program. The VP:

- Reports to the Chief Operating Officer for day-to-day operational oversight.
- Has a dotted-line reporting relationship to the Chair of the Board Sustainability Committee for governance and strategic matters.
- Is a member of the BRHI Executive Leadership Team.
- Oversees the Sustainability and Environmental Compliance Department, which includes the Director of Environmental Compliance, Director of Sustainability Programs, six Regional Environmental Managers, and a team of environmental engineers, analysts, and program managers.
- Has the authority to halt operations at any BRHI facility in the event of an imminent environmental hazard, pending consultation with the COO and Legal Counsel.
- Presents quarterly environmental performance updates to the COO and the Board Sustainability Committee.
- Leads the annual EMS management review.

### 14.3 Annual Management Review

The annual EMS management review (described in Section 3.5) serves as the principal governance mechanism for reviewing and directing BRHI's environmental management system. In addition, the following governance activities occur on a regular basis:

- **Monthly:** Environmental performance metrics are reviewed by the VP of Sustainability and the Director of Environmental Compliance. Any areas of concern are escalated to the COO.
- **Quarterly:** The Board Sustainability Committee reviews a comprehensive environmental performance report, including KPIs, incident reports, audit findings, regulatory compliance status, and progress toward targets.
- **Annually:** The full Board of Directors reviews and approves the annual Sustainability Report and the updated environmental strategy and targets.
- **Biennially:** An independent third-party review of BRHI's environmental governance structure and effectiveness is conducted by an external advisory firm, with findings reported to the Board Sustainability Committee.

### 14.4 Environmental Budget and Resources

BRHI allocates dedicated financial and human resources to support the implementation of this policy:

- **Capital expenditure:** An annual sustainability capital budget is established as part of the corporate capital plan. The budget funds energy efficiency projects, renewable energy installations, EV charging infrastructure, water conservation projects, and waste management improvements. For fiscal year 2026, the sustainability capital budget is $85 million.
- **Operating expenditure:** The Sustainability and Environmental Compliance Department's annual operating budget covers staffing, training, consulting, auditing, regulatory compliance costs, and program management.
- **Innovation fund:** A $10 million annual Sustainability Innovation Fund supports pilot projects and emerging technologies that have the potential to accelerate BRHI's environmental goals, including advanced battery storage, green hydrogen, circular economy business models, and next-generation refrigerants.
- **External funding:** BRHI actively pursues federal, state, and utility incentive programs to offset the cost of environmental investments, including IRA tax credits for solar and storage, EPA grants for fleet electrification, and utility energy efficiency rebates.
