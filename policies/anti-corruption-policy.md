## Policy Statement

BuildRight Home Improvement, Inc. and its wholly owned subsidiaries (collectively, "BRHI," "the Company," "BuildRight," "our," or "we") maintain an absolute and unwavering commitment to conducting business with integrity, transparency, and in full compliance with all applicable anti-corruption, anti-bribery, and fair competition laws in every jurisdiction where we operate.

BRHI has zero tolerance for corruption, bribery, and anti-competitive behavior in any form. No associate, officer, director, contractor, agent, vendor, or business partner of the Company is authorized to offer, promise, give, solicit, or accept bribes, kickbacks, or improper advantages of any kind — whether in dealings with government officials or in commercial transactions with private parties.

This commitment extends beyond legal compliance. We hold ourselves to the highest ethical standards because corruption and anti-competitive conduct undermine free markets, distort fair competition, erode public trust, damage reputations, and harm the communities we serve. Every person associated with BRHI is expected to act in a manner that upholds these principles at all times.

Violations of this policy will result in prompt disciplinary action, up to and including immediate termination of employment or business relationship, and may result in criminal prosecution and civil liability for the individuals involved.

## 2. Anti-Bribery and Anti-Corruption

### 2.1 Legal Framework

BRHI's anti-bribery and anti-corruption program is designed to comply with, and in many respects exceed, the requirements of the following laws and regulations:

#### 2.1.1 Foreign Corrupt Practices Act (FCPA)

The Foreign Corrupt Practices Act of 1977, as amended (15 U.S.C. § 78dd-1 et seq.), is a United States federal law that prohibits:

- **Anti-Bribery Provisions** — any offer, promise, authorization, or payment of money or anything of value to a foreign government official, foreign political party or official thereof, candidate for foreign political office, or any person while knowing that all or a portion of such payment will be offered, given, or promised to any of the foregoing, for the purpose of:
  - Influencing any act or decision of the foreign official in their official capacity.
  - Inducing the foreign official to do or omit to do any act in violation of their lawful duty.
  - Securing any improper advantage.
  - Obtaining or retaining business for or with, or directing business to, any person.

- **Accounting Provisions** — requires companies with securities listed in the United States to maintain books, records, and accounts that, in reasonable detail, accurately and fairly reflect the transactions and dispositions of assets of the company, and to devise and maintain a system of internal accounting controls sufficient to provide reasonable assurances that transactions are executed in accordance with management's authorization, recorded properly, and accessible.

The FCPA applies to BRHI as a U.S.-listed company, its officers, directors, employees, agents, and stockholders acting on behalf of the company, and to any person or entity that takes any act in furtherance of a corrupt payment while within the territory of the United States.

#### 2.1.2 UK Bribery Act 2010

The UK Bribery Act 2010 is one of the most comprehensive anti-bribery statutes in the world and applies to BRHI and its associates to the extent that any act or omission forming part of a bribery offense occurs in the United Kingdom or is committed by a person with a close connection to the UK (including bodies incorporated in the UK). The Act creates four principal offenses:

- **Bribing Another Person (Section 1)** — offering, promising, or giving a financial or other advantage to another person with the intention of inducing that person to perform improperly a relevant function or activity, or as a reward for improper performance.
- **Being Bribed (Section 2)** — requesting, agreeing to receive, or accepting a financial or other advantage with the intention that a relevant function or activity should be performed improperly, or where the acceptance itself constitutes improper performance.
- **Bribery of Foreign Public Officials (Section 6)** — offering, promising, or giving a financial or other advantage to a foreign public official with the intention of influencing the official in their capacity as a foreign public official, and where the official is not permitted by applicable written law to be influenced.
- **Failure of Commercial Organizations to Prevent Bribery (Section 7)** — a commercial organization is guilty of an offense if a person associated with it bribes another person intending to obtain or retain business or a business advantage for the organization, unless the organization can demonstrate that it had in place "adequate procedures" designed to prevent bribery.

The UK Bribery Act's reach is significantly broader than the FCPA in several respects: it prohibits commercial bribery (bribery between private parties), it does not require a corrupt intent or "quid pro quo" for certain offenses, it criminalizes the act of requesting or agreeing to receive a bribe, and it creates strict corporate liability for failure to prevent bribery by associated persons.

#### 2.1.3 Other Applicable Laws

BRHI also complies with all other applicable anti-corruption and anti-bribery laws in jurisdictions where it operates, including but not limited to:

- The OECD Convention on Combating Bribery of Foreign Public Officials in International Business Transactions.
- The Inter-American Convention Against Corruption.
- The African Union Convention on Preventing and Combating Corruption.
- Local anti-corruption statutes in every state, province, and country where BRHI has operations.
- The U.S. Domestic Bribery Statutes (18 U.S.C. § 201 et seq.).
- The U.S. Travel Act (18 U.S.C. § 1952).

### 2.2 Definition of "Anything of Value"

For purposes of this policy, "anything of value" is interpreted broadly and encompasses anything that has monetary value or that could be perceived as conferring a personal benefit. This includes, but is not limited to:

- **Cash and Cash Equivalents** — currency, wire transfers, checks, money orders, gift cards, stored-value cards, prepaid cards, cryptocurrency, and any other instrument that functions as cash or can be readily converted to cash.
- **Gifts** — tangible items of any kind, including merchandise, electronics, luxury goods, clothing, jewelry, alcohol, tobacco products, and collectibles.
- **Meals and Entertainment** — dining at restaurants, attendance at sporting events, concerts, theater, cultural events, nightclub visits, golf outings, spa treatments, and similar entertainment or hospitality.
- **Travel and Accommodation** — flights, hotel stays, transportation, vacation packages, resort stays, cruises, and any related expenses.
- **Employment and Internship Offers** — offers of employment, internships, consulting engagements, or other positions of remuneration, whether to the individual directly or to their family members, friends, or associates.
- **Loans and Guarantees** — personal loans, loan guarantees, or other financial accommodations made on terms not available in the open market.
- **Charitable Donations and Sponsorships** — contributions to charitable organizations, foundations, or causes that are directed by or benefit a particular individual or their interests.
- **Political Contributions** — donations to political parties, political action committees, candidates for public office, or related entities.
- **Favors and Services** — the provision of any personal favor, service, or accommodation, including assistance with visas, licenses, permits, or personal matters.
- **Business Opportunities** — offers of investment opportunities, partnerships, equity stakes, or other business ventures on preferential terms.
- **Discounts and Rebates** — discounts, rebates, or credits not offered in the ordinary course of business to similarly situated customers or vendors.
- **Educational Benefits** — payment of tuition, conference fees, training costs, or other educational expenses for an individual or their family members, except as part of a legitimate, pre-approved BRHI training program.
- **Use of Property or Facilities** — use of BRHI property, vehicles, aircraft, vacation properties, or facilities for personal benefit.

This list is illustrative, not exhaustive. If there is any question about whether something constitutes "anything of value" under this policy, associates should consult the Ethics and Compliance Office before proceeding.

### 2.3 Prohibited Conduct

The following conduct is strictly prohibited under this policy, without exception:

#### 2.3.1 Offering or Giving Bribes

No covered person shall, directly or indirectly, offer, promise, authorize, or give anything of value to any person — whether a government official or a private individual — for the purpose of:

- Influencing any act, decision, or omission by that person in their official or professional capacity.
- Inducing that person to violate their lawful duty or professional obligations.
- Securing an improper business advantage.
- Obtaining or retaining business, contracts, licenses, permits, approvals, or any other benefit.
- Influencing the outcome of a judicial, administrative, or regulatory proceeding.
- Evading or circumventing any legal requirement or regulatory obligation.

#### 2.3.2 Soliciting or Accepting Bribes

No covered person shall, directly or indirectly, solicit, request, demand, agree to receive, or accept anything of value for themselves or for any other person:

- In exchange for taking or omitting to take any official or professional action.
- As a condition for awarding business, providing favorable terms, or granting any BRHI benefit.
- As a reward for having taken or omitted to take any official or professional action.
- To influence any business decision, procurement process, or contract award.

#### 2.3.3 Kickbacks

No covered person shall offer, pay, solicit, or accept any kickback. A kickback is the return of a portion of a payment, contract value, or other benefit to a person as a reward for referring, directing, or securing business. Kickbacks are prohibited regardless of whether they are characterized as commissions, referral fees, consulting payments, or any other description.

#### 2.3.4 Use of Intermediaries

No covered person shall use any intermediary, agent, consultant, or third party to perform any act that would be prohibited if performed directly. Associates may not willfully ignore or deliberately avoid knowledge of corrupt payments made by third parties on BRHI's behalf. Under the FCPA, "knowing" includes conscious disregard and deliberate ignorance of circumstances that would confirm the existence of a violation.

### 2.4 Facilitation Payments

Facilitation payments (also known as "grease payments") — small payments made to low-level government officials to expedite or secure the performance of routine, non-discretionary governmental actions — are strictly prohibited under this policy, regardless of whether such payments may be permitted under the FCPA's limited exception.

BRHI prohibits facilitation payments for the following reasons:

- They are illegal under the UK Bribery Act and the laws of many other jurisdictions.
- Even where technically permitted under local law, they create a culture of corruption and an expectation of payments.
- They expose the Company to reputational harm and regulatory scrutiny.
- They are inconsistent with BRHI's commitment to the highest ethical standards.

In the event that a government official demands a facilitation payment, associates must:

1. Refuse the demand politely but firmly.
2. Request that the official process the matter through normal channels.
3. Ask to speak with the official's supervisor if the demand persists.
4. Document the incident in detail, including the date, location, identity of the official (if known), nature of the demand, and circumstances.
5. Report the incident to the Ethics and Compliance Office within 24 hours.

### 2.5 Commercial Bribery

BRHI prohibits commercial bribery in all forms. Commercial bribery involves the offer, promise, giving, soliciting, or acceptance of anything of value to influence the actions of a private individual or entity in a business context, including but not limited to:

- Paying or receiving bribes to influence the award of contracts by private companies.
- Offering or accepting improper payments in connection with procurement decisions.
- Providing or receiving kickbacks in exchange for business referrals.
- Bribing employees of private companies to obtain confidential information, favorable pricing, or preferential treatment.
- Offering or accepting anything of value to influence the outcome of a private commercial dispute or negotiation.

Commercial bribery is a criminal offense under the UK Bribery Act, the U.S. Travel Act, and the domestic laws of many U.S. states and foreign jurisdictions. Even where not criminally prohibited, commercial bribery violates this policy and BRHI's Code of Business Conduct and Ethics.

### 2.6 Extortion

BRHI recognizes that associates may face situations where they are subjected to extortion — that is, a direct threat to life, safety, or liberty if a payment is not made.

**Immediate Safety First:** If an associate faces an imminent threat to their physical safety or the safety of others, they should comply with the demand to protect life and limb. BRHI will not discipline any associate who makes a payment under a genuine threat of physical harm.

Following such an incident, the associate must:

1. Ensure their immediate safety and the safety of those around them.
2. Contact local law enforcement if appropriate.
3. Notify their immediate supervisor as soon as practicable.
4. Report the incident to the Ethics and Compliance Office within 24 hours.
5. Document the circumstances of the extortion in detail.

Economic extortion — where a government official or business partner threatens to withhold a legitimate benefit, license, or contract unless a payment is made — does not constitute a threat to physical safety and is not an exception to this policy. Associates must report economic extortion demands to the Ethics and Compliance Office immediately and must not make any payment without prior authorization from the Chief Ethics and Compliance Officer or the General Counsel.

## 3. Interaction with Government Officials

### 3.1 Definition of Government Official

For purposes of this policy, "government official" is defined broadly and includes:

- Any officer, employee, or representative of any government, government department, or government agency, at any level (federal, national, state, provincial, regional, municipal, or local).
- Any officer, employee, or representative of any government-owned or government-controlled entity, including state-owned enterprises, sovereign wealth funds, and government-sponsored corporations.
- Any person acting in an official capacity for or on behalf of any government or government entity.
- Any officer or employee of a public international organization, such as the United Nations, the World Bank, the International Monetary Fund, or any similar body.
- Any member of a royal family or ruling household.
- Any political party, political party official, or candidate for political office.
- Any person who holds or is seeking a legislative, administrative, or judicial position, whether elected or appointed.
- Any individual who holds themselves out as having the authority to act on behalf of a government entity, regardless of formal title or status.
- Consultants, advisors, or intermediaries who represent or act on behalf of any of the foregoing.

Associates should be aware that in many countries, individuals who appear to be private sector employees may in fact be government officials because the entity is wholly or partially government-owned or controlled. When there is any doubt about whether an individual qualifies as a government official, the presumption should be that they do, and the Ethics and Compliance Office should be consulted for guidance.

### 3.2 Pre-Approval Requirements

All interactions with government officials that involve business matters on behalf of BRHI must be pre-approved by the Legal Department. This includes, but is not limited to:

- Meetings with government officials to discuss regulatory matters, permits, licenses, or approvals.
- Communications with government officials regarding pending or potential legislation or regulation.
- Participation in government-sponsored events, conferences, or consultations.
- Engaging government officials as speakers, consultants, or advisors.
- Any proposed payment, reimbursement, or benefit to a government official, regardless of amount or purpose.

Associates must submit a written request for pre-approval to the Legal Department at least five (5) business days before the proposed interaction, unless exigent circumstances require a shorter timeline. The request must include:

- The identity and title of the government official.
- The purpose and agenda of the interaction.
- The date, time, and location.
- Any proposed expenditures (gifts, meals, travel, lodging, etc.).
- The business justification for the interaction.

### 3.3 Gifts, Meals, and Entertainment for Government Officials

No gift, meal, or entertainment of any kind shall be provided to any government official without the prior written approval of the Legal Department. This prohibition applies regardless of the value of the item or the circumstances.

In evaluating requests for approval, the Legal Department will consider:

- Whether the gift, meal, or entertainment is consistent with applicable local law, including any local anti-bribery statutes and the laws governing the government official's conduct.
- Whether the gift, meal, or entertainment is reasonable in amount and appropriate in nature.
- Whether the gift, meal, or entertainment serves a legitimate and transparent business purpose.
- Whether the gift, meal, or entertainment could create an appearance of impropriety or undue influence.
- The frequency of gifts, meals, or entertainment provided to the same official.

Even when approved, all gifts, meals, and entertainment provided to government officials must be:

- Modest in value and appropriate to the business occasion.
- Directly related to a legitimate business purpose.
- Fully documented and recorded accurately in the Company's books and records.
- Reported to the Ethics and Compliance Office for inclusion in the Company's gift register.

### 3.4 Political Contributions

#### 3.4.1 Corporate Political Contributions

BRHI shall not make any corporate political contributions — including direct or indirect contributions, donations, loans, advances, or anything of value — to any political party, political committee, candidate for public office, or political action committee without the express prior approval of the Board of Directors.

Requests for corporate political contributions must be submitted to the Board through the Chief Ethics and Compliance Officer and the General Counsel, who will evaluate the request for legal compliance and consistency with Company values.

#### 3.4.2 Individual Political Contributions

BRHI respects the right of its associates to participate in the political process as individual citizens. However:

- No associate may make a personal political contribution and seek or accept reimbursement from BRHI, directly or indirectly.
- No associate may use BRHI funds, resources, facilities, or property to support any political candidate, party, or cause.
- No associate may use their position or authority to coerce or pressure other associates to make political contributions.
- Associates must understand that personal political contributions will not protect them from adverse employment actions, nor will the absence of a contribution subject them to adverse treatment.

#### 3.4.3 Political Action Committees

Any BRHI-affiliated political action committee (PAC) operates independently of this policy and is governed by its own bylaws, a separate Board-approved charter, and all applicable federal and state election laws. The PAC's activities are subject to oversight by the Government Relations Department and the Legal Department.

### 3.5 Lobbying Compliance

All lobbying activities conducted on behalf of BRHI must comply with the Lobbying Disclosure Act of 1995, as amended, the Honest Leadership and Open Government Act of 2007, and all applicable state, local, and foreign lobbying registration and disclosure laws.

- All lobbyists engaged by BRHI must be registered as required by law.
- All lobbying activities must be reported in accordance with applicable disclosure requirements.
- All lobbying expenses must be accurately recorded in the Company's books and records.
- Associates who engage in lobbying activities must coordinate with the Government Relations Department and the Legal Department to ensure compliance.

## 4. Gifts and Entertainment

### 4.1 General Principles

The exchange of business courtesies — including gifts, meals, and entertainment — can serve a legitimate business purpose when conducted appropriately and in moderation. However, such exchanges can also create the appearance of impropriety, influence business judgment, or constitute bribery if not carefully controlled.

All gifts and entertainment given or received in connection with BRHI business must:

- Serve a legitimate, bona fide business purpose.
- Be reasonable in value and appropriate in nature.
- Comply with all applicable laws and Company policies.
- Be given or received transparently, never in secret.
- Be accurately documented and recorded.
- Never be offered, given, or accepted in a manner that could create an appearance of impropriety or influence business decisions.

### 4.2 Gifts and Entertainment Received from Third Parties

Associates may accept gifts and entertainment from third parties (vendors, suppliers, customers, business partners, etc.) only within the following limits:

#### 4.2.1 Monetary Thresholds

- **Per-Gift Limit** — No individual gift may exceed a fair market value of **$50**.
- **Annual Aggregate Limit** — The total value of all gifts received from the same source in any calendar year may not exceed **$100**.
- **Cash and Cash Equivalents** — Cash, gift cards, prepaid cards, stored-value cards, cryptocurrency, and other cash equivalents may **never** be accepted, regardless of amount.

#### 4.2.2 Reporting Requirements

All gifts received with a fair market value of **$25 or more** must be reported to the associate's immediate manager within five (5) business days of receipt. The manager must maintain a written record of the report and ensure the gift is entered into the Company's gift register maintained by the Ethics and Compliance Office.

#### 4.2.3 Perishable Items

Perishable items (e.g., food baskets, flowers) with a fair market value of $50 or less may be accepted without pre-approval but must be reported if valued at $25 or more. Perishable items that exceed $50 in value should be declined and, if the associate is unable to decline without causing offense, shared with the broader team, donated, or reported to the Ethics and Compliance Office for guidance.

#### 4.2.4 Entertainment and Events

Associates may attend business meals, sporting events, cultural events, or similar entertainment hosted by a third party only if:

- The event has a legitimate business purpose.
- The event is attended by representatives of both BRHI and the hosting party.
- The value is reasonable and consistent with customary business practices.
- The associate's manager is informed in advance.
- Attendance would not create an appearance of impropriety or compromise the associate's ability to act in BRHI's best interest.

### 4.3 Gifts and Entertainment Given to Third Parties

Associates may provide gifts and entertainment to third parties only within the following limits:

#### 4.3.1 Monetary Thresholds

- **Per-Gift Limit** — No individual gift may exceed a fair market value of **$50**.
- **Annual Aggregate Limit** — The total value of all gifts given to the same recipient in any calendar year may not exceed **$200**.
- **Pre-Approval Requirement** — Any gift or entertainment with a value exceeding **$25** must receive pre-approval from the associate's manager and the Ethics and Compliance Office before it is given.
- **Government Officials** — No gift, meal, or entertainment of any value may be provided to a government official without prior written approval from the Legal Department, as described in Section 3.3.

#### 4.3.2 Meals and Entertainment Given

Meals and entertainment provided to third parties must:

- Have a clear and bona fide business purpose.
- Be reasonable in cost and appropriate to the business occasion.
- Be attended by at least one BRHI representative.
- Include proper and complete documentation, including the date, location, amount, business purpose, names of all attendees, and their affiliations.
- Be accurately recorded in the Company's expense reporting system under an appropriate category.

#### 4.3.3 Prohibited Gifts and Entertainment

The following are strictly prohibited:

- Cash or cash equivalents in any form or amount.
- Lavish, extravagant, or ostentatious events or gifts.
- Gifts or entertainment that could be perceived as an attempt to influence a pending business decision, contract award, or regulatory matter.
- Gifts or entertainment provided during an active procurement process, bid evaluation, or contract negotiation involving the recipient, unless approved in writing by the Legal Department.
- Any gift or entertainment that violates the laws or policies of the recipient's organization.
- Gifts or entertainment of a personal, intimate, or inappropriate nature.

### 4.4 Tracking and Reporting

The Ethics and Compliance Office maintains a centralized **Gift Register** in which all gifts given and received in connection with BRHI business are recorded. The Gift Register includes:

- The date the gift was given or received.
- The name and affiliation of the giver and recipient.
- A description of the gift or entertainment.
- The estimated fair market value.
- The business purpose or occasion.
- The approval obtained (if applicable).

All associates are responsible for ensuring that gifts and entertainment they give or receive are promptly and accurately reported to the Ethics and Compliance Office for inclusion in the Gift Register. The Ethics and Compliance Office reviews the Gift Register on a quarterly basis to identify patterns, anomalies, or potential policy violations.

## 5. Third-Party Due Diligence

### 5.1 Risk-Based Approach

BRHI employs a risk-based approach to third-party due diligence. The depth and scope of due diligence conducted on a third party is proportional to the level of corruption risk associated with the relationship. All third parties that will interact with government officials on BRHI's behalf, that operate in high-risk jurisdictions, or that perform high-risk functions are subject to enhanced due diligence.

### 5.2 Risk Tiering

Third parties are classified into three risk tiers based on a comprehensive assessment of the following risk factors:

- **Geographic Risk** — The country or countries in which the third party operates, with particular attention to jurisdictions with high Corruption Perceptions Index scores, limited rule of law, or known corruption challenges.
- **Functional Risk** — The nature of the services the third party will provide, including whether the third party will interact with government officials, facilitate regulatory approvals, or perform procurement functions.
- **Transactional Risk** — The size, complexity, and duration of the engagement or transaction.
- **Industry Risk** — Whether the third party operates in an industry or sector with known corruption risks (e.g., construction, extractive industries, government contracting).
- **Relationship Risk** — Whether the third party has personal, familial, or financial connections to government officials.
- **Reputational Risk** — Whether the third party has been the subject of adverse media reports, regulatory actions, or criminal investigations.

#### 5.2.1 Low-Risk Tier

Third parties operating in low-corruption-risk jurisdictions, performing routine commercial functions, and with no identified red flags. Due diligence for low-risk third parties includes:

- Verification of business registration and legal standing.
- Screening against global sanctions, debarment, and law enforcement databases.
- Basic background check on principals and beneficial owners.
- Review of publicly available information and media.
- Completion of the BRHI Third-Party Anti-Corruption Questionnaire.

#### 5.2.2 Medium-Risk Tier

Third parties that present moderate corruption risk due to geographic location, functional role, or other identified risk factors. Due diligence for medium-risk third parties includes all low-risk measures plus:

- Enhanced background checks on principals, beneficial owners, and key personnel.
- Verification of business reputation and references.
- Review of the third party's anti-corruption policies and compliance programs.
- Assessment of compensation structure and reasonableness of fees.
- Screening for connections to government officials.

#### 5.2.3 High-Risk Tier

Third parties that present elevated corruption risk, including those that will interact with government officials on BRHI's behalf, operate in jurisdictions with systemic corruption, or have been flagged through the risk assessment process. Due diligence for high-risk third parties includes all low- and medium-risk measures plus:

- Engagement of an independent third-party due diligence firm to conduct a comprehensive investigation.
- In-depth review of the third party's ownership structure, beneficial ownership, and ultimate controlling persons.
- On-site visit and assessment of the third party's operations and compliance infrastructure.
- Detailed review of financial records, including source of funds and compensation arrangements.
- Interview of key personnel regarding their understanding of and commitment to anti-corruption compliance.
- Ongoing monitoring plan with defined review milestones and escalation triggers.

### 5.3 Contractual Anti-Corruption Provisions

All agreements with third parties must include anti-corruption representations, warranties, and covenants appropriate to the risk tier. Standard provisions include:

- **Representation and Warranty** — The third party represents and warrants that it has not and will not, in connection with its engagement by BRHI, offer, promise, give, or authorize any payment or anything of value to any government official or private individual for an improper purpose.
- **Compliance Covenant** — The third party covenants to comply with all applicable anti-corruption and anti-bribery laws, including the FCPA and the UK Bribery Act.
- **Policy Compliance** — The third party acknowledges receipt of this policy and agrees to conduct its activities on behalf of BRHI in accordance with its terms.
- **Certification** — The third party agrees to certify on an annual basis (or more frequently if requested) that it has complied with the anti-corruption provisions of the agreement.
- **Cooperation** — The third party agrees to cooperate fully with any investigation or audit conducted by BRHI or its designees.
- **Books and Records** — The third party agrees to maintain accurate books and records related to its engagement by BRHI and to make such records available for review.

### 5.4 Right to Audit

BRHI reserves the right to audit any third party's books, records, and operations to the extent they relate to the third party's engagement by BRHI. Third parties are required to cooperate fully with any such audit and to provide timely access to all relevant documents, personnel, and facilities.

Audits may be conducted by BRHI's Internal Audit Department, the Ethics and Compliance Office, or an independent third-party auditor engaged by BRHI. The costs of the audit are borne by BRHI unless the audit reveals a violation, in which case the third party shall reimburse BRHI for all audit costs.

### 5.5 Ongoing Monitoring

Third-party relationships are subject to ongoing monitoring throughout the life of the engagement. This includes:

- Periodic re-assessment of the third party's risk tier (at least annually, or more frequently if circumstances warrant).
- Review of the third party's annual anti-corruption compliance certification.
- Monitoring of publicly available information, including media reports and sanctions lists.
- Review of expense reports and invoices for unusual or suspicious charges.
- Periodic audits of high-risk third parties.
- Prompt investigation of any red flags or concerns identified.

### 5.6 Termination for Violation

BRHI will terminate its relationship with any third party that violates this policy, the anti-corruption provisions of its agreement with BRHI, or any applicable anti-corruption law. Termination may be effected immediately and without notice for material violations. The Company will also report the violation to appropriate law enforcement and regulatory authorities as required by law.

## 6. Fair Competition and Antitrust

### 6.1 Legal Framework

BRHI is committed to full compliance with all applicable antitrust and competition laws, which are designed to protect the competitive process and ensure that markets operate efficiently for the benefit of consumers. The principal laws governing fair competition in BRHI's operations include:

- **Sherman Antitrust Act** (15 U.S.C. §§ 1–7) — prohibits contracts, combinations, and conspiracies in restraint of trade (Section 1) and monopolization, attempted monopolization, and conspiracy to monopolize (Section 2).
- **Clayton Antitrust Act** (15 U.S.C. §§ 12–27) — addresses specific practices that the Sherman Act does not explicitly prohibit, including mergers and acquisitions that may substantially lessen competition, exclusive dealing arrangements, tying arrangements, and interlocking directorates.
- **Federal Trade Commission Act** (15 U.S.C. §§ 41–58) — prohibits unfair methods of competition and unfair or deceptive acts or practices.
- **Robinson-Patman Act** (15 U.S.C. § 13) — prohibits price discrimination that substantially lessens competition.
- **State Antitrust Laws** — virtually every U.S. state has its own antitrust statutes that may impose additional requirements and provide for private enforcement.
- **International Competition Laws** — BRHI complies with the competition laws of all jurisdictions in which it operates, including the Competition Act (Canada), the Competition Act 1998 (UK), and the Treaty on the Functioning of the European Union (Articles 101 and 102).

### 6.2 Prohibited Conduct

The following anti-competitive conduct is strictly prohibited under this policy:

#### 6.2.1 Price Fixing

No associate shall enter into any agreement, understanding, or arrangement — whether formal or informal, written or oral, express or implied — with a competitor regarding:

- The prices at which either party will sell products or services.
- Pricing methodologies, formulas, or components.
- Discounts, rebates, credit terms, or financing arrangements.
- The timing or manner of price changes or price announcements.
- Profit margins, markups, or rate of return.
- Any other term or condition of sale that affects price.

Price fixing is a per se violation of the Sherman Act, meaning it is automatically unlawful regardless of its actual effect on competition, the parties' intent, or any claimed business justification. Criminal penalties for price fixing include fines of up to $100 million for corporations and up to $1 million and ten years' imprisonment for individuals.

#### 6.2.2 Bid Rigging

No associate shall engage in any form of bid rigging, including but not limited to:

- **Bid Suppression** — agreeing with a competitor that one party will refrain from bidding or will withdraw a bid to allow another party to win.
- **Complementary Bidding** — submitting a deliberately high bid or a bid with terms designed to be unacceptable, so that a designated competitor wins the contract.
- **Bid Rotation** — taking turns being the lowest bidder on a series of contracts.
- **Market Division** — agreeing with a competitor to allocate customers, territories, or product lines, with each party agreeing not to compete for the other's allocated share.
- **Subcontracting** — agreeing that the winning bidder will subcontract work to losing bidders as part of a pre-arranged scheme.

Bid rigging is a per se violation of the Sherman Act and is subject to the same severe criminal penalties as price fixing.

#### 6.2.3 Market Allocation

No associate shall enter into any agreement, understanding, or arrangement with a competitor to:

- Divide or allocate markets, territories, or customer lists.
- Agree not to solicit each other's customers.
- Allocate specific product lines or categories of business.
- Refrain from expanding into new markets or geographic areas.

#### 6.2.4 Group Boycotts

No associate shall participate in any agreement or concerted action with a competitor or group of competitors to:

- Refuse to deal with a particular supplier, customer, or other market participant.
- Coerce a supplier, customer, or other market participant to adhere to particular terms or conditions.
- Exclude a competitor from access to a market, supply source, or distribution channel.

### 6.3 Interaction with Competitors

Associates must exercise particular caution when interacting with competitors, whether at trade association meetings, industry conferences, social events, or any other setting.

#### 6.3.1 Trade Association Guidelines

Participation in trade associations is generally lawful and can serve legitimate business interests, such as industry standard-setting, education, and advocacy. However, trade association meetings present a heightened risk of unlawful communications with competitors. Associates attending trade association meetings must:

- Review and comply with BRHI's Trade Association Participation Guidelines.
- Be alert to any discussion that touches on pricing, costs, margins, output, capacity, customer allocation, territories, or business plans.
- Immediately and clearly object to any improper discussion and, if it continues, excuse themselves from the meeting.
- Prepare a written memorandum documenting any improper discussion, including the date, location, participants, and nature of the discussion, and submit it to the Legal Department within 24 hours.
- Ensure that all trade association meetings have a written agenda distributed in advance and formal minutes prepared afterward.

#### 6.3.2 Prohibited Information Exchange

Associates shall not exchange, discuss, or share with competitors any competitively sensitive information, whether directly or through an intermediary, including:

- Current, past, or future prices, pricing policies, or pricing strategies.
- Costs, cost structures, or cost components.
- Profit margins, markups, or profitability data.
- Production capacity, output levels, or inventory data.
- Customer lists, customer information, or customer allocation.
- Territorial or geographic market plans.
- Individual company business plans, strategies, or forecasts.
- Bid or proposal information for any pending or anticipated competitive process.
- Compensation data for employees (except through properly structured industry surveys compiled by independent third parties).

#### 6.3.3 Independent Decision-Making

All pricing, marketing, distribution, and business strategy decisions must be made independently by BRHI, based solely on the Company's own assessment of market conditions, costs, competitive dynamics, and customer needs. Associates must never allow communications with competitors — whether direct or indirect — to influence the Company's independent business judgment.

### 6.4 Pricing Decisions

Pricing is one of the most sensitive areas of antitrust law. The following rules govern BRHI's pricing practices:

- All pricing decisions must be made independently, without any agreement or understanding with competitors.
- Associates must not communicate with competitors about prices, pricing plans, or pricing strategies under any circumstances.
- Price surveys or benchmarking must use only publicly available information or data provided by independent third parties, and must be reviewed by the Legal Department before use.
- Price changes must be implemented independently and without prior notification to competitors (except through ordinary public announcements that are available to all customers simultaneously).
- Internal discussions about competitor pricing must be based on publicly available information only and must be documented with the source of the information.

### 6.5 Exclusive Dealing and Tying Arrangements

Exclusive dealing arrangements (requiring a customer or supplier to deal exclusively with BRHI) and tying arrangements (requiring a customer to purchase one product as a condition of purchasing another) may raise antitrust concerns under certain circumstances.

- All proposed exclusive dealing or tying arrangements must be reviewed and approved by the Legal Department before implementation.
- The Legal Department will evaluate the arrangement for compliance with applicable antitrust laws, including an assessment of BRHI's market power in the relevant markets and the potential competitive effects.
- Associates must not propose or enter into any exclusive dealing or tying arrangement without prior written legal approval.

### 6.6 Vendor and Supplier Relationships

BRHI is committed to dealing fairly with its vendors and suppliers. In managing vendor and supplier relationships:

- All procurement decisions must be based on objective criteria, including quality, price, service, reliability, and value.
- No vendor or supplier shall be excluded or disadvantaged based on anti-competitive agreements or understandings with competitors.
- BRHI shall not abuse any market power it may have in a particular market to impose unfair terms on vendors or suppliers.
- Vendor negotiations must be conducted in good faith and without coercion, intimidation, or deceptive practices.
- All vendor and supplier agreements must include appropriate antitrust and fair competition compliance provisions.

### 6.7 Mergers and Acquisitions

All mergers, acquisitions, joint ventures, and other business combinations involving BRHI must comply with applicable antitrust laws, including the Hart-Scott-Rodino Antitrust Improvements Act of 1976 (HSR Act).

- The Legal Department must be consulted at the earliest practicable stage of any proposed transaction.
- Pre-merger notification filings under the HSR Act must be completed before the transaction can proceed, where applicable.
- The Legal Department will coordinate with outside antitrust counsel to assess the competitive implications of the proposed transaction and to obtain any required antitrust clearances.
- No associate shall communicate with competitors about any proposed transaction without the prior written approval of the Legal Department.
- Associates must preserve all documents related to any proposed transaction in accordance with the Legal Department's instructions.

## 7. Charitable Contributions and Sponsorships

### 7.1 General Principles

BRHI supports charitable causes and community organizations as part of its commitment to being a responsible corporate citizen. However, charitable contributions and sponsorships must be bona fide and must never be used as a vehicle for bribery or improper influence.

### 7.2 Pre-Approval Requirements

- All charitable contributions and sponsorships exceeding **$1,000** (whether a single contribution or an aggregate of contributions to the same organization in a calendar year) must receive prior written approval from the Ethics and Compliance Office.
- All charitable contributions and sponsorships, regardless of amount, that involve or benefit organizations affiliated with government officials must receive prior written approval from the Legal Department.

### 7.3 Due Diligence on Recipient Organizations

Before any charitable contribution or sponsorship is approved, the Ethics and Compliance Office will conduct due diligence on the recipient organization, including:

- Verification of the organization's tax-exempt status and legal standing (e.g., 501(c)(3) status in the United States).
- Screening against global sanctions and watch lists.
- Review of the organization's mission, activities, and reputation.
- Identification of the organization's officers, directors, and principal donors.
- Assessment of any connections between the organization and government officials who have authority over matters affecting BRHI.
- Evaluation of whether the contribution could be perceived as an attempt to influence a government official or secure an improper business advantage.

### 7.4 Prohibitions

The following are strictly prohibited:

- Charitable contributions made at the direction or request of a government official, unless approved by the Legal Department and the Chief Ethics and Compliance Officer.
- Contributions to organizations where a government official serves as an officer, director, or principal, without Legal Department approval.
- Contributions that are intended to, or could reasonably be perceived as, an attempt to obtain or retain business, secure a regulatory benefit, or reward a government official.
- Contributions used as a conduit to transfer funds to a government official or their family members.
- Sponsorships of events or organizations where the primary beneficiary is a government official or their associates.
- Contributions made in exchange for a commitment or expectation of favorable official action.

### 7.5 Recordkeeping

All charitable contributions and sponsorships must be accurately recorded in the Company's books and records, including the identity of the recipient, the amount, the date, the business or charitable purpose, and the approval obtained. Supporting documentation must be retained in accordance with the Company's Records Retention Policy (BRHI-POL-004).

## 8. Books and Records

### 8.1 Accuracy Requirements

All transactions, regardless of amount or nature, must be accurately and fairly recorded in BRHI's books, records, and accounts. This requirement applies to all financial records, including but not limited to:

- General ledger entries.
- Accounts payable and accounts receivable records.
- Expense reports and reimbursement requests.
- Invoice processing and payment records.
- Journal entries and adjusting entries.
- Contracts and amendments.
- Purchase orders and procurement records.
- Payroll and compensation records.

### 8.2 Prohibited Practices

The following practices are strictly prohibited:

- Maintaining off-book accounts, slush funds, or undisclosed cash reserves.
- Recording transactions in a manner that conceals their true nature, purpose, or beneficiary.
- Entering into transactions that are not properly documented or authorized.
- Making false, misleading, or artificial entries in the Company's books and records.
- Failing to record transactions promptly and in the correct accounting period.
- Describing expenses in vague, misleading, or generic terms (e.g., "consulting services" or "special expenses") when the true nature of the expenditure is different.
- Creating or maintaining documentation that does not accurately reflect the substance of the underlying transaction.

### 8.3 Expense Reporting

All expenses must be described with sufficient particularity to identify the true business purpose and nature of the expenditure. Expense reports must include:

- The date and location of the expenditure.
- A specific description of the purpose of the expense.
- The names and affiliations of all individuals involved (e.g., attendees at a business meal).
- Original receipts and supporting documentation.
- The correct cost center, project code, or general ledger account.

Associates must not mischaracterize expenses or use vague descriptions to obscure the true nature of a payment. If an associate is unsure about how to properly record an expense, they should consult the Finance Department or the Ethics and Compliance Office.

### 8.4 Document Retention

All books, records, and supporting documentation must be retained in accordance with BRHI's Records Retention Policy (BRHI-POL-004). Associates must not destroy, alter, or conceal any document or record that is or may become relevant to an investigation, audit, or legal proceeding.

## 9. Internal Controls

### 9.1 Segregation of Duties

BRHI maintains a robust system of internal controls that includes appropriate segregation of duties to prevent and detect errors, irregularities, and fraud. No single individual should have control over all aspects of any significant financial transaction. Key segregation requirements include:

- Separation of transaction initiation, approval, execution, and recording functions.
- Separation of custody of assets from recordkeeping responsibilities.
- Independent review and approval of expenditures above defined thresholds.

### 9.2 Approval Authority Limits

BRHI maintains a formal approval authority matrix that defines the levels of approval required for different types and amounts of expenditures. Key provisions include:

- All expenditures must be approved by an authorized individual in accordance with the approval authority matrix.
- No associate may approve an expenditure for their own benefit or for the benefit of their direct or indirect interests.
- Approvals must be documented in writing (including electronic approvals) and retained in accordance with the Company's record retention policies.
- Exceptions to the approval authority matrix must be approved in writing by the next higher level of authority and reported to the Ethics and Compliance Office.

### 9.3 Monitoring and Testing

The Ethics and Compliance Office, in coordination with the Internal Audit Department, conducts regular monitoring and testing of the Company's anti-corruption controls, including:

- Quarterly review of the Gift Register for completeness, accuracy, and compliance with policy thresholds.
- Sampling and testing of expense reports, particularly in high-risk categories (consulting fees, travel, entertainment, government relations).
- Review of third-party due diligence files for completeness and timeliness.
- Testing of procurement processes for compliance with competitive bidding requirements.
- Monitoring of charitable contribution and sponsorship approvals and supporting documentation.

### 9.4 Internal Audit Review

The Internal Audit Department includes anti-corruption and fair competition compliance as a standing component of its annual audit plan. Internal audit activities include:

- Risk-based audits of business units, geographic regions, and functional areas with elevated corruption or antitrust risk.
- Review of the design and operating effectiveness of anti-corruption internal controls.
- Testing of high-risk transactions and third-party relationships.
- Evaluation of compliance with this policy and related procedures.
- Reporting of audit findings to the Audit Committee of the Board of Directors and the Chief Ethics and Compliance Officer.

## 10. Reporting and Whistleblower Protection

### 10.1 Duty to Report

Every person covered by this policy has a duty to promptly report any known, suspected, or reasonably anticipated violation of this policy, any applicable anti-corruption or antitrust law, or any other indication of potential corruption, bribery, or anti-competitive conduct. Reports should be made as soon as practicable after the reporter becomes aware of the concern.

### 10.2 Reporting Channels

BRHI provides multiple channels for reporting concerns, and reporters may use any channel they prefer:

- **Ethics Hotline** — 1-800-BUILD-RIGHT (1-800-284-5337), available 24 hours a day, 7 days a week, 365 days a year. The hotline is operated by an independent third-party provider and supports reporting in over 200 languages. Reporters may choose to remain anonymous where permitted by local law.
- **Web Portal** — Reports may be submitted online at ethics.buildright.com. The web portal is hosted by an independent third-party provider and allows for anonymous reporting and secure follow-up communication.
- **Direct to Compliance** — Reports may be made directly to the Ethics and Compliance Office by email (compliance@buildright.com), telephone, or in person.
- **Direct to Legal** — Reports may be made directly to the Legal Department or the General Counsel.
- **Direct to Human Resources** — Reports may be made to any Human Resources representative.
- **Management Reporting** — Reports may be made to any member of management, who is then responsible for promptly escalating the report to the Ethics and Compliance Office.

### 10.3 Non-Retaliation Policy

BRHI strictly prohibits retaliation against any person who, in good faith, reports a known or suspected violation of this policy, any Company policy, or any law. Retaliation includes any adverse employment action — including termination, demotion, suspension, threats, harassment, discrimination, reduction in hours or pay, reassignment, or any other action intended to punish or discourage reporting.

Any person who engages in retaliation against a good-faith reporter will be subject to disciplinary action, up to and including immediate termination of employment or business relationship.

This non-retaliation protection applies regardless of whether the reported concern is ultimately substantiated, as long as the report was made in good faith.

### 10.4 Confidentiality

BRHI will treat all reports confidentially to the greatest extent possible, consistent with the need to conduct an adequate investigation and comply with applicable law. The identity of the reporter will be disclosed only on a need-to-know basis and will not be disclosed to the subject of the report.

### 10.5 Incentives for Reporting

BRHI encourages associates to come forward with concerns and recognizes the importance of early detection and reporting. In addition to the protections described above, the Company may provide positive recognition or incentives to associates who identify significant compliance risks or violations through good-faith reporting, as determined by the Ethics and Compliance Office.

## 11. Investigation Process

### 11.1 Initiation

Upon receipt of a report of a known or suspected violation of this policy, the Ethics and Compliance Office will initiate a preliminary assessment within **48 hours**. The purpose of the preliminary assessment is to:

- Determine whether the report alleges conduct that, if true, would constitute a violation of this policy or applicable law.
- Assess the seriousness and credibility of the allegation.
- Identify any immediate risks requiring interim action (e.g., suspension of a business relationship, preservation of documents).
- Determine the appropriate scope and methodology for a full investigation.

### 11.2 Investigation Team

If the preliminary assessment warrants further investigation, the Chief Ethics and Compliance Officer will assemble an investigation team. The team may include:

- Members of the Ethics and Compliance Office.
- Attorneys from the Legal Department (or outside counsel, where appropriate to ensure independence).
- Members of the Internal Audit Department.
- Subject matter experts as needed (e.g., forensic accountants, IT security specialists).
- Outside investigators or forensic specialists for complex or sensitive matters.

The investigation team operates under the direction of the Chief Ethics and Compliance Officer, with oversight by the General Counsel and, as appropriate, the Audit Committee of the Board of Directors.

### 11.3 Document Preservation

Upon initiation of an investigation, the Legal Department will issue a document preservation notice to all individuals and business units that may possess relevant documents, records, or electronically stored information. All recipients of such a notice must:

- Preserve all potentially relevant documents and data in their possession or control.
- Refrain from destroying, altering, or concealing any document or data.
- Refrain from deleting any email, electronic files, or other records that may be relevant.
- Cooperate fully with the investigation team's requests for documents and information.

Failure to comply with a document preservation notice may result in disciplinary action and may expose the individual and the Company to legal liability.

### 11.4 Cooperation with Authorities

BRHI is committed to cooperating fully with government investigations, regulatory inquiries, and law enforcement requests related to potential violations of anti-corruption or antitrust laws. All such inquiries and requests must be immediately reported to the General Counsel, who will coordinate the Company's response. No associate may obstruct, delay, or interfere with any government investigation or legal proceeding.

### 11.5 Investigation Outcomes

Upon completion of an investigation, the investigation team will prepare a written report summarizing the findings, conclusions, and recommendations. The report will be provided to the Chief Ethics and Compliance Officer, the General Counsel, and, as appropriate, the Audit Committee of the Board of Directors.

### 11.6 Discipline for Violations

Violations of this policy will result in prompt and appropriate disciplinary action, which may include:

- Verbal or written warning.
- Mandatory additional training.
- Reduction in compensation or elimination of bonus.
- Suspension with or without pay.
- Demotion or reassignment.
- Termination of employment for cause.
- Termination of business relationship (for third parties).
- Referral to law enforcement or regulatory authorities for criminal prosecution or civil enforcement.

The severity of the discipline will depend on the nature, severity, and circumstances of the violation, the individual's level of responsibility, whether the violation was intentional, whether the individual self-reported, and the individual's cooperation with the investigation. Termination of employment will be the presumptive outcome for intentional violations, violations involving government officials, and violations resulting in significant financial benefit to the individual or harm to the Company.

## 12. Training and Certification

### 12.1 Annual Mandatory Training

All associates must complete anti-corruption and fair competition training within **30 days** of their hire date and annually thereafter. Training will cover:

- The requirements of this policy.
- The FCPA, the UK Bribery Act, and other applicable anti-corruption laws.
- U.S. federal and state antitrust laws and international competition laws.
- How to identify and respond to bribery, corruption, and anti-competitive situations.
- The Company's reporting channels and non-retaliation protections.
- Recent enforcement trends and case studies.
- The consequences of non-compliance.

Training will be delivered through a combination of online modules, live presentations, and interactive workshops. Completion will be tracked through the Company's learning management system.

### 12.2 Enhanced Training for High-Risk Roles

Associates in the following high-risk roles must complete enhanced training in addition to the standard annual training:

- **Procurement and Supply Chain** — associates involved in vendor selection, contract negotiation, and purchasing decisions.
- **International Operations** — associates who work in or have responsibility for operations outside the United States.
- **Government Liaisons** — associates who interact with government officials on behalf of the Company.
- **Sales and Business Development** — associates involved in customer-facing sales, contract bidding, and business development activities.
- **Finance and Accounting** — associates responsible for maintaining the Company's books and records and processing payments.
- **Legal and Compliance** — associates in the Legal Department and Ethics and Compliance Office.
- **Executive Leadership** — officers and directors of the Company.

Enhanced training will include scenario-based exercises, role-specific guidance, and more detailed coverage of applicable laws and regulations.

### 12.3 Third-Party Training

BRHI requires all high-risk and medium-risk third parties to complete anti-corruption training as a condition of engagement. Training must be completed within 30 days of engagement and annually thereafter. BRHI will provide training materials or, where appropriate, require the third party to certify completion of an equivalent training program approved by the Ethics and Compliance Office.

### 12.4 Annual Certification

All associates must execute an annual written certification confirming that they:

- Have read, understood, and agree to comply with this policy.
- Have completed the required training.
- Are not aware of any unreported violations of this policy (or have reported any known or suspected violations through the Company's reporting channels).
- Understand the consequences of non-compliance.

Certifications will be collected and maintained by the Ethics and Compliance Office. Failure to complete the annual certification by the specified deadline will result in escalation to the associate's management chain and may result in disciplinary action.

## 13. Monitoring and Auditing

### 13.1 Annual Risk Assessment

The Ethics and Compliance Office conducts a comprehensive anti-corruption and fair competition risk assessment at least annually. The risk assessment evaluates:

- The Company's geographic footprint and the corruption and competition risks associated with each jurisdiction.
- The nature and scope of the Company's interactions with government officials.
- The volume and risk profile of third-party relationships.
- Transaction data and trends, including high-value, unusual, or anomalous transactions.
- Industry enforcement trends and regulatory developments.
- Internal and external reports, hotline data, and investigation outcomes.
- Findings from internal audits and control testing.

The results of the risk assessment inform the allocation of compliance resources, the design of monitoring and testing activities, and updates to this policy.

### 13.2 Internal Audit Program

The Internal Audit Department maintains a standing audit program for anti-corruption and fair competition compliance. The program includes:

- Scheduled audits of business units and functional areas based on the annual risk assessment.
- Targeted audits triggered by specific concerns, reports, or risk indicators.
- Post-transaction reviews of significant contracts, acquisitions, and joint ventures.
- Review of the Company's third-party due diligence program for effectiveness and consistency.
- Testing of the Company's gift, entertainment, and hospitality controls.

Audit results are reported to the Audit Committee of the Board of Directors and the Chief Ethics and Compliance Officer.

### 13.3 Transaction Testing

The Ethics and Compliance Office conducts regular transaction testing, including:

- Sampling and review of payments to agents, consultants, and intermediaries.
- Review of expense reports for gifts, entertainment, travel, and hospitality expenditures.
- Analysis of charitable contribution and sponsorship records.
- Review of procurement and vendor payment records for anomalies.
- Testing of pricing decisions for evidence of anti-competitive coordination.
- Review of government-related expenditures and interactions.

### 13.4 Continuous Monitoring of High-Risk Transactions

The Company employs automated monitoring tools and analytics to continuously identify and flag high-risk transactions for review. These include:

- Payments to or in high-risk jurisdictions.
- Unusual payment patterns or structures.
- Payments to third parties that lack a clear business justification.
- Invoices with vague or incomplete descriptions.
- Round-dollar payments or other anomalies.
- Transactions with government-related entities.
- Expense reports that exceed policy thresholds.

Flagged transactions are reviewed by the Ethics and Compliance Office and, where warranted, escalated for further investigation.

## 14. Consequences of Non-Compliance

### 14.1 Individual Consequences

Violations of anti-corruption and antitrust laws carry severe consequences for the individuals involved, including:

- **Employment Consequences** — termination of employment, damage to professional reputation, loss of professional licenses, and difficulty securing future employment.
- **Criminal Penalties** — under the FCPA, individuals may face fines of up to $250,000 per violation and imprisonment of up to five years; under the UK Bribery Act, individuals may face imprisonment of up to ten years; under U.S. antitrust laws, individuals may face fines of up to $1 million and imprisonment of up to ten years.
- **Civil Liability** — individuals may be subject to personal civil liability, including disgorgement of profits, damages, and injunctions.
- **Regulatory Consequences** — debarment from government contracting, exclusion from regulated industries, and loss of professional certifications.

### 14.2 Corporate Consequences

Violations of anti-corruption and antitrust laws also carry severe consequences for BRHI as a company, including:

- **Criminal Fines** — under the FCPA, corporate fines can reach $2 million per violation (with many violations charged in a single case, resulting in aggregate fines of hundreds of millions of dollars); under U.S. antitrust laws, corporate fines can reach $100 million per violation; actual settlements in major anti-corruption and antitrust cases have reached billions of dollars.
- **Civil Penalties and Disgorgement** — the SEC may impose civil monetary penalties and require disgorgement of ill-gotten profits, plus prejudgment interest.
- **Debarment and Exclusion** — conviction of certain offenses can result in debarment from government contracting, exclusion from government programs, and loss of export privileges, any of which could have a devastating impact on BRHI's business.
- **Deferred Prosecution and Non-Prosecution Agreements** — the Company may be required to enter into agreements with enforcement agencies that impose costly compliance monitorships, reporting obligations, and ongoing oversight.
- **Private Litigation** — antitrust violations expose the Company to treble-damages actions by private plaintiffs, which can result in judgments exponentially larger than the actual damages.
- **Reputational Damage** — public disclosure of enforcement actions can cause lasting and irreparable harm to the Company's brand, customer relationships, vendor relationships, and ability to attract and retain talent.
- **Imprisonment for Individuals** — the Company's officers, directors, and associates may face imprisonment for their involvement in violations, as emphasized above.

### 14.3 Shared Responsibility

Compliance with this policy is the responsibility of every person covered by it. Ignorance of the law, pressure from supervisors, direction from management, or the belief that "everyone does it" are not defenses to violations of anti-corruption or antitrust laws. Every individual is personally accountable for their conduct.
