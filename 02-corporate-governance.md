## 2. Board of Directors

### 2.1 Composition and Size

The Board of Directors (the "Board") of BuildRight Home Improvement, Inc. ("BRHI" or the "Company") shall consist of 12 directors. The Board may increase or decrease the size of the Board by resolution, provided that the Board shall at all times have a minimum of 3 and a maximum of 15 directors.

### 2.2 Independence Standards

A majority of the directors shall at all times be independent as determined under the listing standards of the New York Stock Exchange ("NYSE") and the Company's own heightened independence criteria.

A director is considered independent only if the Board affirmatively determines that the director has no material relationship with the Company, either directly or as a partner, shareholder, or officer of an organization that has a relationship with the Company. In making this determination, the Board applies the following criteria:

**No director shall be deemed independent if:**

- The director has been an employee of the Company, or has had an immediate family member who has been an executive officer of the Company, within the preceding three (3) years.
- The director has received, or has an immediate family member who has received, during any twelve-month period within the preceding three years, more than $120,000 in direct compensation from the Company (other than director and committee fees and pension or other forms of deferred compensation for prior service, provided such compensation is not contingent in any way on continued service).
- The director is a current partner or employee of a firm that is the Company's internal or external auditor, or has an immediate family member who is a current partner of such firm or who was a partner or employee of such firm and personally worked on the Company's audit within the preceding three years.
- The director is employed, or has an immediate family member who is employed, as an executive officer of another company where any of the Company's present executive officers at the same time serve on that company's compensation committee, creating interlocking directorates.
- The director is an executive officer or employee, or has an immediate family member who is an executive officer, of a company that makes payments to, or receives payments from, the Company for property or services in an amount which, in any single fiscal year, exceeds the greater of $1 million or 2% of such other company's consolidated gross revenues (material commercial relationship exceeding $120,000 per year threshold).

### 2.3 Director Qualifications and Skills Matrix

The Board maintains a skills matrix to ensure the collective body possesses the diverse perspectives and expertise necessary to oversee the Company's strategic direction. The Nominating & Governance Committee evaluates candidates against the following qualification areas:

- **Retail and Consumer Products Experience** — Direct experience in multi-unit retail operations, merchandising, consumer packaged goods, or home improvement industries
- **Financial Literacy and Expertise** — Ability to read and understand fundamental financial statements, including the balance sheet, income statement, and cash flow statement; at least one director per committee shall have advanced financial expertise
- **Technology and Digital Transformation** — Experience in e-commerce, enterprise technology, data analytics, cybersecurity, or digital marketing
- **Supply Chain and Operations** — Experience in logistics, distribution, manufacturing, sourcing, or supply chain management
- **Executive Leadership** — Current or prior service as a CEO, COO, or president of a significant enterprise
- **Corporate Governance** — Experience serving on public company boards, familiarity with SEC regulations, and understanding of fiduciary duties
- **Risk Management** — Experience identifying and managing enterprise-level risks, including operational, financial, and cyber risks
- **Legal and Regulatory** — Familiarity with the regulatory environment applicable to large retail operations
- **Sustainability and ESG** — Knowledge of environmental stewardship, social responsibility, and sustainable business practices
- **DEI Perspective** — Deep understanding of diversity, equity, and inclusion principles, including workforce diversity, equitable practices, and inclusive leadership

### 2.4 Diversity, Equity, and Inclusion Targets

The Board is committed to fostering diversity among its membership. The Nominating & Governance Committee shall ensure that the Board includes a minimum of two (2) directors who are diverse as defined by gender, race, ethnicity, or other underrepresented backgrounds. The Committee shall actively seek diverse candidates for every open Board seat and shall engage diverse candidate slates from executive search firms.

### 2.5 Election and Voting Standard

In uncontested elections, each director shall be elected by the affirmative vote of a majority of the votes cast with respect to such director at any meeting of shareholders for the election of directors at which a quorum is present. "Majority of votes cast" means that the number of votes cast "for" a director's election exceeds the number of votes cast "against" that director's election.

Any director who fails to receive a majority of votes cast in an uncontested election shall promptly tender his or her resignation to the Board. The Nominating & Governance Committee shall make a recommendation to the Board on whether to accept or reject the resignation, and the Board shall act on the resignation within 90 days following certification of the election results. The Board's decision shall be publicly disclosed.

In contested elections (where the number of nominees exceeds the number of directors to be elected), the voting standard shall be a plurality of votes cast.

### 2.6 Tenure and Retirement Policy

- **Age Limit:** No director shall be renominated or re-elected to the Board after reaching the age of 75, unless the Board, upon recommendation of the Nominating & Governance Committee, determines that an exception is warranted due to extraordinary circumstances. Any such exception must be approved by a two-thirds vote of the independent directors.
- **Tenure Limit for Independent Directors:** No independent director shall serve for more than fifteen (15) years. Upon reaching the 15-year tenure limit, the director shall rotate off the Board at the expiration of his or her current term. The Nominating & Governance Committee may grant a one-year extension if the Committee determines that the director's continued service is in the best interests of the Company.
- **Board Refreshment:** The Nominating & Governance Committee shall conduct an annual assessment of Board composition, including average tenure, skills coverage, diversity, and refreshment needs, and shall report its findings and recommendations to the full Board.

### 2.7 Annual Board Self-Evaluation

The Board shall conduct a comprehensive self-evaluation on an annual basis, typically in the fourth fiscal quarter. The evaluation process consists of two components:

1. **Written Questionnaire** — Each director completes a confidential written questionnaire covering the following areas:
   - Board effectiveness in fulfilling its oversight responsibilities
   - Quality and timeliness of meeting materials and pre-reads
   - Adequacy of time spent on strategy versus operational matters
   - Quality of discussions and decision-making processes
   - Committee effectiveness and workload distribution
   - Board composition, skills, and diversity
   - Management performance and relationship with the Board
   - Suggestions for improvement

2. **Executive Session Discussion** — Following completion and aggregation of the questionnaires, the independent directors meet in executive session to discuss the results, identify areas for improvement, and develop action plans. The Lead Independent Director facilitates this discussion.

The Nominating & Governance Committee oversees the evaluation process and may engage a third-party governance consultant to facilitate the evaluation every three years. Results are summarized and presented to the full Board, with specific action items tracked and reported at subsequent meetings.

### 2.8 Lead Independent Director

The independent directors shall elect a Lead Independent Director from among their number by majority vote. The Lead Independent Director shall serve a term of one year and may be re-elected for additional terms without limitation. The Lead Independent Director's responsibilities include:

- Presiding at all executive sessions of the independent directors
- Approving meeting agendas for the full Board, in consultation with the Chair of the Board and CEO
- Approving meeting schedules to ensure adequate time for discussion of all agenda items
- Reviewing and approving the content and distribution of Board meeting materials
- Serving as a liaison between the independent directors and the Chair of the Board and CEO
- Being available for consultation and direct communication with major shareholders, and responding to shareholder inquiries regarding governance matters
- Calling meetings of the independent directors when deemed necessary
- Recommending to the Board the retention of independent advisors and consultants
- Participating in the CEO performance evaluation process
- Coordinating with committee chairs on matters of mutual interest

### 2.9 Director Compensation

The following compensation structure applies to non-management directors of the Board:

- No management director (i.e., a director who is also an executive officer of the Company) receives any compensation for Board or committee service beyond his or her executive compensation package.
- The Company maintains a charitable matching program under which the Company matches up to $10,000 per year in charitable contributions made by each director.

## 3. Board Committees

### 3.1 Audit Committee

| Attribute | Requirement |
|---|---|
| **Size** | Minimum 3 members |
| **Independence** | All members must be independent directors |
| **Financial Expertise** | At least 1 member must be a CPA or have equivalent financial expertise as defined by SEC rules |
| **Meeting Frequency** | 8 times per year (minimum), with additional meetings as circumstances require |

**Charter Duties and Responsibilities:**

**Financial Reporting Oversight:**
- Review and discuss with management and the external auditor the Company's annual audited financial statements (Form 10-K) and quarterly unaudited financial statements (Form 10-Q) prior to filing
- Review and discuss earnings press releases, including the use of any non-GAAP financial measures, before public dissemination
- Review major issues regarding accounting principles, financial statement presentations, and critical audit matters
- Discuss with management and the external auditor the adequacy and effectiveness of the Company's internal controls over financial reporting, including any significant deficiencies or material weaknesses
- Receive and review management's assessment of significant financial risks and contingencies

**Internal Audit Oversight:**
- Approve the appointment and removal of the Vice President of Internal Audit
- Review and approve the annual internal audit plan, including scope, budget, and staffing
- Receive quarterly reports from the VP of Internal Audit summarizing audit findings, recommendations, and management's corrective action plans
- Review the effectiveness of the internal audit function, including its independence, authority, and reporting relationships
- Ensure the internal audit function has unrestricted access to all Company records, personnel, and physical locations

**External Auditor Oversight:**
- Appoint, retain, compensate, evaluate, and, where appropriate, terminate the engagement of the independent registered public accounting firm (the "external auditor")
- Pre-approve all audit services and permitted non-audit services to be provided by the external auditor, in accordance with the pre-approval policy and schedule:
  - **Audit Services:** Annual integrated audit of financial statements and ICFR, quarterly reviews, comfort letters, statutory audits
  - **Audit-Related Services:** Due diligence for acquisitions, employee benefit plan audits, agreed-upon procedures, internal control assessments
  - **Tax Services:** Tax compliance, tax planning, and tax advisory services, subject to specific pre-approval thresholds ($500K per engagement for recurring compliance, any amount for advisory requires Committee approval)
  - **All Other Services:** Prohibited unless specifically approved by the Committee on a case-by-case basis
- Review and discuss with the external auditor the audit scope, plan, and schedule
- Receive and review the external auditor's reports on critical audit matters, significant deficiencies, material weaknesses, and any illegal acts
- Evaluate the external auditor's qualifications, performance, and independence annually, including a review of the auditor's quality control report and any PCAOB inspection findings
- Review and discuss with the external auditor all relationships that may bear on the auditor's independence, including the auditor's confirmation of independence
- Establish and enforce policies for the Company's hiring of employees or former employees of the external auditor (one-year cooling-off period for audit engagement team members)

**Whistleblower Program:**
- Oversee the Company's whistleblower program and the integrity of the confidential ethics hotline
- Receive quarterly reports on all hotline cases, including the number and nature of complaints, investigation status, and resolutions
- Review procedures for the receipt, retention, and treatment of complaints regarding accounting, internal accounting controls, or auditing matters
- Ensure that no retaliatory action is taken against any person who submits a good-faith complaint

**SOX 302/404 Compliance:**
- Oversee management's compliance with Sections 302 and 404 of the Sarbanes-Oxley Act of 2002
- Review the CEO's and CFO's quarterly certifications under SOX 302
- Review management's annual assessment of the effectiveness of internal controls over financial reporting (ICFR) under SOX 404
- Review the external auditor's attestation report on management's ICFR assessment
- Monitor remediation of any identified significant deficiencies or material weaknesses
- Review management's disclosure committee charter and procedures

**Risk Management Oversight:**
- Oversee the Company's risk management processes as they relate to financial risks, including credit risk, liquidity risk, interest rate risk, and foreign currency risk
- Review the Company's cybersecurity risk management program, including the adequacy of cybersecurity controls, incident response plans, and business continuity plans
- Discuss with management the Company's major financial risk exposures and the steps management has taken to monitor and control such exposures
- Coordinate with the Risk & Compliance Committee on enterprise-wide risk matters

**Related-Party Transactions:**
- Review and approve or ratify all related-party transactions required to be disclosed under Item 404 of Regulation S-K
- Apply the Company's Related Party Transaction Policy (see Section 4.4) to all such transactions
- Review procedures for the identification and disclosure of related-party transactions

### 3.2 Compensation & Human Capital Committee

| Attribute | Requirement |
|---|---|
| **Size** | Minimum 3 members |
| **Independence** | All members must be independent directors |
| **Meeting Frequency** | 6 times per year (minimum) |

**Charter Duties and Responsibilities:**

**Executive Compensation:**
- Set the compensation for the Chief Executive Officer, including base salary, annual incentive (short-term incentive), and long-term equity incentive awards, based on evaluation of the CEO's performance against established goals
- Review and approve compensation for the Chief Financial Officer and other Named Executive Officers (NEOs), including base salary, annual incentive targets, and long-term equity awards
- Review and recommend to the Board for approval all equity-based compensation plans and any material amendments thereto
- Review and approve the annual incentive plan design, including performance metrics, target levels, and payout curves, for all NEOs
- Review and approve the long-term incentive plan design, including the mix of equity vehicles (restricted stock units, performance shares, stock options), performance conditions, and vesting schedules
- Evaluate the CEO's performance annually against the Company's strategic objectives and financial targets, in consultation with the independent directors meeting in executive session

**Clawback Policy Administration:**
- Administer the Company's Clawback Policy (see Section 4.6), including determinations regarding applicability, amounts subject to recovery, and any exceptions
- Review and approve any modifications to the Clawback Policy to ensure compliance with Dodd-Frank Section 953(b), SEC Rule 10D-1, and applicable exchange listing standards

**Equity Plan Administration:**
- Review and approve all grants of equity awards under the Company's equity incentive plans, including individual grant amounts, award types, vesting schedules, and performance conditions
- Review and approve any modifications, accelerations, or cancellations of equity awards
- Monitor equity plan utilization, including shares available for grant, burn rate, and overhang
- Approve the use of any equity awards in connection with new hire offers, retention, or other special circumstances for NEOs and other designated officers

**Associate Compensation Philosophy:**
- Review and approve the Company's overall compensation philosophy, ensuring it supports the Company's strategy to attract, retain, and motivate high-performing associates
- Review market benchmarking data and competitive positioning of associate compensation programs
- Monitor associate turnover rates and engagement survey results as they relate to compensation competitiveness

**Human Capital Management:**
- Oversee the Company's human capital management strategies, policies, and programs, including:
  - **Diversity, Equity, and Inclusion:** Review DEI metrics, goals, and progress, including representation at all levels of the organization, pay equity analyses, and inclusive leadership initiatives
  - **Talent Management:** Oversee talent acquisition, development, and retention programs, including succession planning for key leadership roles
  - **Employee Engagement:** Review results of annual and pulse engagement surveys and management's action plans to address identified issues
  - **Workforce Planning:** Assess the Company's workforce planning and development strategies, including upskilling and reskilling initiatives
  - **Total Rewards:** Review the design and competitiveness of benefits programs, including health and wellness, retirement, and work-life balance offerings

**Say-on-Pay:**
- Review and recommend to the Board the resolution to be included in the Company's annual proxy statement seeking the advisory vote of shareholders on the compensation of NEOs ("Say-on-Pay" vote)
- Review the results of the Say-on-Pay vote and consider shareholder feedback in future compensation decisions
- Recommend to the Board the frequency of the Say-on-Pay vote (annual, biennial, or triennial) at least once every six years

**Independent Compensation Consultant:**
- Retain and terminate the engagement of an independent compensation consultant to assist the Committee in evaluating executive compensation, including the consultant's independence assessment under SEC and NYSE rules
- Approve the consultant's fees and other retention terms
- Receive and review the consultant's analysis of competitive compensation practices, market trends, and pay-for-performance alignment
- The consultant shall report directly to the Committee and shall provide no other services to the Company's management without prior Committee approval

## 4. Corporate Governance Policies

### 4.1 Corporate Governance Guidelines

The following guidelines govern the operation and practices of the Board of Directors:

**Board Leadership Structure:**

The Board does not have a fixed policy on whether the roles of Chair of the Board and Chief Executive Officer should be separate or combined, and reserves the flexibility to make this determination based on the Company's circumstances at any given time. When the Chair is not an independent director, the Board shall elect a Lead Independent Director with the duties described in Section 2.8.

**Independent Director Meetings:**

The independent directors shall meet in executive session without management present at every regularly scheduled Board meeting. Additional executive sessions may be called by the Lead Independent Director at any time. The Lead Independent Director presides at all executive sessions.

**Director Access to Management and Independent Advisors:**

Directors have full and free access to all officers and associates of the Company. Any director may contact any Company associate without prior approval from management. The Board and each committee have the authority to retain, terminate, and approve the fees and retention terms of independent legal, financial, accounting, or other advisors as they deem necessary in the discharge of their responsibilities, without consulting or obtaining the approval of any officer of the Company.

**Board Materials Distribution:**

Meeting materials, including the agenda, financial reports, committee reports, and supporting documentation, shall be distributed to directors at least five (5) business days before each scheduled Board meeting. Management shall ensure that materials are clear, comprehensive, and provide sufficient information to enable informed decision-making. Supplemental materials may be distributed closer to the meeting date as circumstances require.

**Meeting Frequency and Schedule:**

The Board shall hold a minimum of six (6) regularly scheduled meetings per year. The annual meeting schedule shall be approved at the organizational meeting following the annual shareholder meeting. Additional special meetings may be called in accordance with the Bylaws.

**Attendance Requirement:**

Each director is expected to attend all scheduled Board meetings and all meetings of committees on which the director serves. A director who attends fewer than 75% of the aggregate of all Board and applicable committee meetings in any fiscal year shall be subject to review by the Nominating & Governance Committee, which may recommend that the director tender his or her resignation.

**Continuing Education:**

The Company shall reimburse up to $15,000 per director per year for costs associated with continuing education programs related to the director's Board or committee service, including corporate governance, financial literacy, industry-specific, and regulatory compliance programs.

### 4.2 Code of Ethics for Senior Financial Officers

This Code of Ethics applies to the following Senior Financial Officers of the Company:

- Chief Executive Officer
- Chief Financial Officer
- Controller (Principal Accounting Officer)
- Any other principal financial officer or senior officer performing a similar function

**Standards of Conduct:**

### 4.3 Insider Trading Policy

**Applicability:**

This Insider Trading Policy applies to all directors, officers, and employees of the Company and its subsidiaries, as well as consultants, contractors, and other individuals who receive material nonpublic information about the Company.

**Blackout Periods:**

No director, officer, or employee subject to this policy shall purchase, sell, or otherwise transact in the Company's securities during the following blackout periods:

- **Regular Blackout Period:** Beginning ten (10) calendar days before the end of each fiscal quarter and ending at the commencement of the second (2nd) full trading day following the public release of the Company's earnings for that quarter
- **Special Blackout Periods:** The General Counsel may declare special blackout periods when, in his or her judgment, the Company is in possession of material nonpublic information that would make trading inappropriate. Affected individuals will be notified by the Corporate Secretary's office

**Section 16 Officers and Directors Pre-Clearance:**

All directors and officers subject to Section 16 of the Securities Exchange Act of 1934 ("Section 16 Insiders") must obtain pre-clearance from the General Counsel before engaging in any transaction involving the Company's securities, including purchases, sales, gifts, option exercises, and transfers to or from trusts. Pre-clearance requests should be submitted at least two (2) business days before the proposed transaction. Pre-clearance is valid for five (5) business days from the date of approval.

**Rule 10b5-1 Plans:**

Section 16 Insiders and designated employees may adopt Rule 10b5-1 trading plans in accordance with the following requirements:

- The plan must be adopted during an open trading window when the individual is not in possession of material nonpublic information
- The plan must specify the amount, price, and date of transactions, or provide a written formula or algorithm for determining these terms
- The individual must not exercise any subsequent influence over the transactions
- A mandatory cooling-off period of thirty (30) calendar days must elapse between the adoption or modification of the plan and the first trade executed under the plan
- Individuals may not adopt more than one Rule 10b5-1 plan at a time (subject to the SEC's limited single-plan exception rules)
- Plan terminations and modifications must be pre-cleared by the General Counsel

**Quarterly Transaction Reporting:**

All Section 16 Insiders must report transactions in Company securities to the Corporate Secretary within one (1) business day of execution to facilitate timely filing of Form 4 with the SEC. All other officers and designated employees must report transactions within five (5) business days.

**Prohibited Transactions:**

In addition to the blackout period restrictions, the following transactions in Company securities are prohibited for all directors, officers, and employees:

- Short sales (selling borrowed shares)
- Trading in options, puts, calls, or other derivative securities on Company stock
- Holding Company securities in a margin account as collateral for a margin loan

### 4.4 Related Party Transaction Policy

**Scope:**

This policy applies to any transaction or series of transactions in which the Company is a participant, the amount involved exceeds $120,000, and a Related Party has or will have a direct or indirect material interest. A "Related Party" is defined as:

- Any executive officer (as defined by SEC rules) of the Company
- Any director of the Company
- Any nominee for director of the Company
- Any immediate family member of any of the above (spouse, parent, child, sibling, mother-in-law, father-in-law, son-in-law, daughter-in-law, brother-in-law, sister-in-law, or any person sharing the household of such individual, other than a tenant or employee)

- The terms of the transaction, including pricing, duration, and any special conditions
- The impact, if any, on the director's independence under NYSE listing standards and the Company's independence criteria
- Whether the transaction would present an improper conflict of interest
- The aggregate dollar amount of the transaction

**Ongoing Monitoring:**

**Disclosure:**

### 4.6 Clawback Policy

**Applicability:**

This Clawback Policy applies to all current and former Named Executive Officers (NEOs) of the Company, as defined by SEC rules. The policy is in addition to, and not in lieu of, any other remedies or recoupment rights available to the Company.

**Triggering Events:**

Recovery of incentive-based compensation shall be required under the following circumstances:

1. **Material Financial Restatement:** If the Company is required to prepare a material accounting restatement of its financial statements due to noncompliance with any financial reporting requirement under the securities laws (a "Big R" restatement), or if the Company would have prepared such a restatement to correct a material error that was not timely identified (a "little r" restatement)
2. **Misconduct:** If the Board determines that an NEO engaged in fraud or intentional misconduct that contributed to, or was a proximate cause of, the need for a material accounting restatement

**Lookback Period:**

The policy applies to all incentive-based compensation received by an NEO during the three (3) fiscal years immediately preceding the date of the accounting restatement trigger. "Incentive-based compensation" includes any compensation that is granted, earned, or vested based wholly or in part upon the attainment of any financial reporting measure.

**Recovery Amount:**

- **Dodd-Frank Section 953(b) Mandatory Recovery:** The recoverable amount is the excess of the incentive-based compensation received by the NEO (before taxes) over the amount that would have been received had the compensation been determined based on the restated financial results
- **SOX Section 304 (CEO/CFO only):** In the event of a restatement due to misconduct, the CEO and CFO shall reimburse the Company for any bonus or other incentive-based or equity-based compensation received during the 12-month period following the first public issuance or filing of the noncompliant document, plus any profits realized from the sale of Company securities during that period

**Recovery Process:**

Upon a triggering event, the Compensation & Human Capital Committee shall:

1. Determine the amount of excess incentive-based compensation subject to recovery
2. Notify the affected NEO in writing of the recovery demand, including the amount and basis for the calculation
3. Provide the NEO with 30 days to respond or propose an alternative repayment arrangement
4. Pursue recovery within 180 days of the Committee's determination, through one or more of the following methods:
   - Direct repayment by the NEO
   - Offset against outstanding or future compensation owed by the Company
   - Cancellation of outstanding equity awards
   - Recovery from proceeds of any sale of Company securities
   - Any other method permitted by applicable law

**No Waiver:**

The Board and the Committee may not waive recovery under this policy for any NEO. The Company shall not indemnify or purchase insurance to cover an NEO's clawback obligation.

## 7. Risk Management Framework

### 7.1 Three Lines of Defense Model

The Company's risk management framework is organized around the internationally recognized Three Lines of Defense model:

**First Line — Business Unit Managers (Risk Owners):**

Business unit managers and operational leaders are responsible for owning and managing risks within their areas of responsibility on a daily basis. This includes:

- Identifying and assessing risks inherent in business processes and activities
- Implementing and maintaining effective internal controls
- Monitoring risk indicators and escalating emerging risks
- Ensuring compliance with applicable policies, procedures, laws, and regulations
- Reporting risk incidents and near-misses in a timely manner
- Executing risk mitigation action plans
- Fostering a risk-aware culture within their teams

**Second Line — Risk Management, Compliance, Legal, HR, and Information Security (Risk Oversight):**

Centralized risk management and compliance functions establish policies, set standards, and provide oversight to ensure the first line is effectively managing risks. This includes:

- Developing and maintaining the enterprise risk management framework and methodology
- Establishing risk appetite and tolerance levels for Board approval
- Setting policies and procedures for risk identification, assessment, and mitigation
- Providing independent challenge and oversight of first-line risk management activities
- Monitoring and reporting on key risk indicators and risk appetite utilization
- Conducting compliance monitoring and testing programs
- Providing risk management training and awareness programs
- Coordinating risk reporting to senior management and the Board

**Third Line — Internal Audit (Independent Assurance):**

The Internal Audit function provides independent and objective assurance to the Audit Committee and management on the effectiveness of governance, risk management, and internal controls. This includes:

- Developing and executing a risk-based annual audit plan
- Evaluating the design and operating effectiveness of internal controls
- Assessing the adequacy of the first and second lines' risk management activities
- Reporting findings, recommendations, and management action plans to the Audit Committee
- Following up on previously identified issues to verify remediation
- Maintaining organizational independence with a direct reporting line to the Audit Committee

### 7.2 Enterprise Risk Management (ERM) Process

The Company follows a structured ERM process that is conducted on an annual basis with ongoing monitoring throughout the year:

**Step 1 — Identify:**

- Annual enterprise-wide risk assessment survey distributed to all senior leaders and functional heads
- One-on-one interviews with 50 or more executives across the organization, including all C-suite officers, vice presidents, and key regional and operational leaders
- Environmental scanning for emerging risks, including industry trends, regulatory developments, geopolitical events, and technological changes
- Analysis of internal and external loss event data, audit findings, and compliance incidents
- Benchmarking against industry peers and published risk reports

**Step 2 — Assess:**

- Each identified risk is evaluated on two dimensions: likelihood of occurrence and potential impact
- Likelihood is assessed on a 5-point scale: (1) Rare — less than 5% probability, (2) Unlikely — 5-20% probability, (3) Possible — 20-50% probability, (4) Likely — 50-80% probability, (5) Almost Certain — greater than 80% probability
- Impact is assessed on a 5-point scale: (1) Insignificant — less than $5M financial impact, (2) Minor — $5M to $25M, (3) Moderate — $25M to $100M, (4) Major — $100M to $500M, (5) Catastrophic — greater than $500M or reputational damage threatening enterprise viability
- Risk scores are calculated as Likelihood multiplied by Impact, producing a composite score from 1 to 25
- Results are plotted on a risk heat map to visualize the risk landscape and prioritize attention on high-severity risks

**Step 3 — Mitigate:**

- For each high and critical risk (score of 12 or above), a designated risk owner is assigned
- Risk owners develop mitigation strategies and action plans, including:
  - Preventive controls to reduce the likelihood of occurrence
  - Detective controls to identify incidents promptly when they occur
  - Corrective actions to minimize impact and restore normal operations
  - Contingency plans for residual risks that cannot be fully mitigated
- Mitigation plans are reviewed and approved by the Management Risk Committee
- Clear timelines and milestones are established for each action plan

**Step 4 — Monitor:**

- Key Risk Indicators (KRIs) are established for each top risk, with defined thresholds (green/amber/red)
- KRIs are reported to the Management Risk Committee on a quarterly basis
- Monthly incident tracking captures operational losses, compliance violations, safety events, cybersecurity incidents, and other risk events
- Threshold breaches and emerging risk trends trigger management investigation and, where warranted, escalation to the Board

**Step 5 — Report:**

- Quarterly risk register updates are presented to the Risk & Compliance Committee of the Board
- Semi-annual comprehensive risk reports are presented to the full Board, including updated risk heat maps, trend analyses, and forward-looking risk assessments
- The annual risk assessment process and results are documented in a formal report available to all Board members and senior management

### 7.3 Risk Appetite Statement

The Board approves the Company's Risk Appetite Statement annually, establishing the broad parameters within which the Company is willing to accept risk in pursuit of its strategic objectives. The Risk Appetite Statement addresses the following categories:

- **Strategic Risk — Accept Measured Risk for Growth:** The Company accepts measured and calculated risks that are aligned with its strategic objectives and have the potential to generate sustainable long-term value. The Company pursues organic growth, strategic acquisitions, and new market entry when the expected risk-adjusted returns exceed the cost of capital. The Company does not accept risks that could jeopardize its competitive position or long-term viability.

- **Financial Risk — Conservative Leverage, Investment-Grade Ratings:** The Company maintains a conservative financial profile and is committed to preserving investment-grade credit ratings (targeting a minimum of BBB+/Baa1). The Company maintains leverage ratios within Board-approved limits (net debt-to-EBITDA not to exceed 3.0x under normal conditions). The Company maintains a minimum liquidity buffer of $2 billion in committed credit facilities and cash on hand.

- **Operational Risk — Minimize Disruption, 99.9% POS Uptime:** The Company seeks to minimize operational disruptions that could affect customer experience, associate safety, or business continuity. The Company maintains a target of 99.9% point-of-sale system uptime across all stores and distribution centers. Supply chain resilience is maintained through diversified sourcing, safety stock strategies, and business continuity plans.

- **Compliance Risk — Zero Tolerance for Intentional Violations:** The Company has zero tolerance for intentional violations of laws, regulations, or Company policies. The Company expects all associates to act with integrity and to comply with both the letter and spirit of applicable laws and regulations. Inadvertent compliance gaps identified through monitoring and audit activities are expected to be remediated promptly.

- **Reputational Risk — Protect Brand Through Ethical Conduct:** The Company recognizes that its reputation is a critical intangible asset and commits to protecting it through ethical conduct, responsible business practices, and transparent stakeholder engagement. The Company does not accept actions or practices that could materially damage its reputation with customers, associates, shareholders, or communities.

## 8. Regulatory Compliance Framework

### 8.1 Sarbanes-Oxley Act (SOX) 302/404

The Company maintains a comprehensive SOX compliance program to ensure the accuracy and integrity of its financial reporting:

**SOX 302 — Corporate Responsibility for Financial Reports:**

- The CEO and CFO certify the accuracy and completeness of all financial statements and disclosures in each quarterly and annual filing (Forms 10-Q and 10-K)
- Certifications confirm that the signing officers have reviewed the report, it does not contain any untrue statement of a material fact, and the financial statements fairly present the financial condition and results of operations
- Certifications address the effectiveness of disclosure controls and procedures
- Any significant deficiencies, material weaknesses, or fraud involving management must be disclosed to the Audit Committee and external auditor

**SOX 404 — Management Assessment of Internal Controls:**

- Management performs an annual assessment of the effectiveness of the Company's internal control over financial reporting (ICFR)
- The assessment is based on the Committee of Sponsoring Organizations of the Treadway Commission (COSO) Internal Control — Integrated Framework (2013)

### 8.3 OSHA Compliance

The Company maintains compliance with the Occupational Safety and Health Act and applicable state workplace safety laws:

- **Workplace Safety Compliance:** All stores, distribution centers, and office facilities are maintained in compliance with applicable OSHA standards, including hazard communication (HazCom), walking-working surfaces, electrical safety, fire protection, and personal protective equipment requirements
- **OSHA 300 Log:** Each establishment required to maintain the OSHA 300 Log of Work-Related Injuries and Illnesses, with entries recorded within 7 calendar days of notification of a recordable injury or illness
- **Annual 300A Posting:** The OSHA 300A Summary of Work-Related Injuries and Illnesses is posted in a conspicuous location at each establishment from February 1 through April 30 annually, and is electronically submitted to OSHA by March 2
- **Fatality Reporting:** Any work-related fatality is reported to OSHA within 8 hours of the incident
- **Hospitalization Reporting:** Any work-related in-patient hospitalization of one or more associates, amputation, or loss of an eye is reported to OSHA within 24 hours of the incident
- **Citation Response:** All OSHA citations are reviewed by the Legal Department, and appropriate corrective actions are implemented within the abatement period. Citations are reported to the Risk & Compliance Committee

### 8.4 EPA Compliance

The Company maintains compliance with the Environmental Protection Agency regulations and applicable state and local environmental laws:

- **Air Emissions:** Facilities with air emission sources maintain applicable permits and comply with emission limits, monitoring, recordkeeping, and reporting requirements under the Clean Air Act
- **Water Discharge:** Facilities with wastewater discharge maintain applicable permits under the Clean Water Act (National Pollutant Discharge Elimination System permits or state equivalents) and comply with discharge limits and monitoring requirements
- **Chemical Reporting (TRI):** Facilities that meet the reporting thresholds file annual Toxic Release Inventory (TRI) reports (Form R) with the EPA by July 1 of each year, covering listed chemicals manufactured, processed, or otherwise used above threshold quantities during the preceding calendar year

## 9. Conflict of Interest Management

### 9.1 Annual Disclosure Requirement

All directors and officers at the level of Vice President and above shall complete an annual Conflict of Interest Disclosure Form. The form requires disclosure of:

- All outside business interests, including directorships, officer positions, or significant ownership interests in any entity that is a competitor, supplier, customer, or potential business partner of the Company
- All relationships with any entity that has a business relationship with the Company, including consulting arrangements, partnerships, or joint ventures
- All family members (spouse, domestic partner, parents, children, siblings, and in-laws) who are employed by or have a significant financial interest in any entity that is a competitor, supplier, customer, or potential business partner of the Company
- Any personal financial interests that could conflict, or appear to conflict, with the individual's duties to the Company
- Any gifts, entertainment, or other benefits received from third parties in excess of $250 in aggregate per year from any single source
- Any outside activities, speaking engagements, or publications that relate to the Company's business

### 9.2 Review Process

- Completed disclosure forms are submitted to the General Counsel by January 31 of each year
- The General Counsel reviews all disclosures and identifies potential or actual conflicts of interest
- Disclosures determined to be non-material are documented and filed
- Material conflicts are reported to the Audit Committee for review and disposition
- The General Counsel shall maintain a confidential register of all disclosed conflicts and the resolution of each

### 9.3 Restrictions

- **Recusal:** Any director or officer who has a conflict of interest with respect to a particular matter shall recuse himself or herself from all deliberations and decisions on that matter, including leaving the meeting room during the relevant discussion
- **No Competitive Business Interests:** No director or officer shall have a financial interest in, or serve as a director, officer, or consultant to, any enterprise that competes directly with the Company in any material line of business, without prior approval from the Board
- **No Misuse of Company Information:** No director or officer shall use or disclose confidential Company information for personal benefit or for the benefit of any person or entity other than the Company
- **No Self-Dealing:** No director or officer shall use his or her position to influence the Company's business decisions for personal financial benefit or the benefit of family members or associated entities

### 9.4 Violations

Failure to disclose a conflict of interest, or failure to comply with the restrictions imposed by this policy, constitutes a violation of the Company's Code of Business Conduct and Ethics and may result in disciplinary action up to and including:

- Termination of employment
- Removal from the Board of Directors
- Recission of transactions entered into in violation of this policy
- Referral for legal proceedings where warranted
